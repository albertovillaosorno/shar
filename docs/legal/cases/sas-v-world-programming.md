## Question Presented

What must a software copyright claimant identify and filter when asserting
protection in nonliteral elements, including input or output formats?

## Verified Holding And Record

The Federal Circuit affirmed judgment for World Programming because SAS had not
identified the specific protectable elements allegedly copied with the
particularity required for infringement analysis. General categories such as
input formats and output designs did not substitute for identifying concrete
expression and filtering out ideas, processes, methods, public-domain material,
merger-bound elements, and scenes a faire.

The opinion treated input and output formats as possible nonliteral software
elements requiring granular analysis. It did not adopt a categorical rule that
all such formats are either protected or unprotected.

## Limits

- The decision applies the governing procedural record and law before the
  Federal Circuit; its general copyright discussion does not automatically bind
  every regional circuit.
- Failure of proof in the case does not establish that every command, schema,
  report design, or format lacks protectable expression.
- Identification and filtration do not resolve substantial similarity,
  ownership, authorization, contract, or other legal regimes.

## Repository Relevance

A legal claim or defense concerning a SHAR format must identify the exact field,
label, ordering, grouping, command, default, or other element at issue. Broad
references to a format, interface, or file family are insufficient for a
reliable protectability analysis.
