# ProCD, Inc. v. Zeidenberg

> [Legal Research Disclaimer](../disclaimer.md) applies to this record.

- Status: Reported Seventh Circuit opinion text verified from a public-domain
  reporter duplicate; an official archival court copy was not located.
- Court: United States Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit.
- Authority: 86 F.3d 1447 (7th Cir. 1996).
- Docket: 96-1139.
- Decision date: 1996-06-20.
- As-of date: 2026-07-16.
- Counsel review: Not performed.

## Question Presented

Could terms presented inside a retail software package form an enforceable
contract, and did 17 U.S.C. § 301 preempt enforcement of the use restriction?

## Verified Holding And Record

The Seventh Circuit held that the reviewed shrink-wrap license was enforceable
unless its terms were objectionable under rules applicable to contracts in
general. The package gave notice that terms were inside, the software displayed
the terms, the license allowed return for a refund, and the buyer used the
product rather than rejecting it.

Applying Wisconsin's Uniform Commercial Code, the court held that a vendor could
propose a transaction in which acceptance occurred after the buyer had an
opportunity to inspect the product and terms. The opportunity to return the
goods was important evidence. The decision did not require every software term
to be printed on the outside of the package or accepted at the cash register.

The court also rejected § 301 preemption on the reviewed contract claim. It
distinguished copyright rights, which bind strangers, from contractual promises,
which generally bind only the parties. Enforcement did not prevent strangers
from independently obtaining and using the uncopyrightable telephone-directory
facts.

The court nevertheless declined to adopt a categorical rule that every claim
labeled `contract` escapes preemption. It recognized that some contract-law
applications could interfere with federal objectives. The holding was limited to
general enforcement of the shrink-wrap agreement before it.

## Limits And Later Context

- The decision applied Wisconsin contract law and the Uniform Commercial Code to
  a specific retail package, notice, displayed terms, refund right, and use.
- The parties did not argue that the reviewed terms were unconscionable or
  otherwise invalid under a generally applicable contract rule.
- The opinion does not establish that hidden terms, unavailable terms, or terms
  presented without a meaningful rejection route are enforceable.
- Later browsewrap and sign-in-wrap cases require their own conspicuous-notice
  and manifestation-of-assent analysis.
- The preemption result does not make every private promise enforceable or
  permit a contract to bind a nonparty.
- Enforcing the promise did not create copyright in uncopyrightable facts and
  did not restrict strangers from independently acquiring them.
- Consumer law, nonwaivable rights, public policy, arbitration, modification,
  choice of law, and jurisdiction remain separate questions.

## Repository Relevance

SHAR cannot infer assent merely from possession of a disc, installation of a
game, public availability of terms, or continued use without reconstructing the
complete transaction. The exact package notice, installer presentation, terms,
return opportunity, account flow, and user action must be preserved.

The opinion also supports a claim-specific preemption analysis. A private
promise may differ from a copyright right against the world, but labels do not
decide the result. The agreement, parties, duty, conduct, remedy, governing law,
and federal objective must all be identified.

## Required Facts

- The package, storefront, installer, account, or download notice.
- When and how the complete terms became available.
- The exact action alleged to accept the terms.
- Whether rejection, cancellation, or return was actually available and usable.
- The governing contract law and applicable consumer protections.
- The exact promise, parties, conduct, remedy, and federal right implicated.
- Amendments, renewed assent, incorporation, and later transaction evidence.
- Later controlling authority in the relevant jurisdiction.
