# MGE UPS Systems, Inc. v. GE Consumer and Industrial, Inc

## Question Presented

Did employees circumvent an access control under § 1201(a)(1) when they used an
already altered software copy, and what proof was required for copyright profits
and injunctive relief?

## Verified Record

MGE produced copyrighted service software used with its uninterruptible-power
systems. In ordinary operation, the software required an authorized external
hardware key before it would fully launch. An unknown source altered at least
one copy so that the key was no longer required.

Employees of the defendant service company later obtained and used the altered
copy. The defendants admitted five instances of use, while MGE alleged many
more. The record did not identify who altered the software or establish that a
representative of the defendants disabled, removed, avoided, or bypassed the
hardware-key requirement.

The jury separately found copyright infringement and trade-secret
misappropriation. The district court dismissed the DMCA claim as a matter of law
but entered damages and a permanent injunction on other claims.

## Circumvention Holding

Section 1201(a)(1) targets the act of circumventing a technological measure. The
statutory definition includes avoiding or bypassing a measure, but the Fifth
Circuit refused to treat every later use of an already cracked copy as a new act
of circumvention.

MGE proved that its control had been defeated at some earlier point, but it did
not prove that the defendants or their representatives performed that act. Their
employees used the software after the unknown alteration. On that record, the
employees' later execution of the altered copy did not itself establish that
they circumvented the hardware-key control.

The court therefore affirmed dismissal of the § 1201(a)(1) claim. The holding is
actor-specific: a claimant must connect the accused party to an act that avoids,
bypasses, removes, deactivates, or impairs the measure, rather than proving only
that the party later used a work whose protection had already been defeated.

## Separate Copyright And Other Claims

The DMCA result did not make the software use lawful. The defendants admitted
instances of direct copyright infringement, and the record supported separate
trade-secret and unfair-competition theories. A failure to prove circumvention
by the accused actor does not resolve reproduction, adaptation, possession,
contract, confidentiality, authorization, or trade-secret questions.

The Fifth Circuit affirmed the permanent injunction. The record included
infringing uses after a preliminary injunction, missing laptops associated with
admitted users, and uncertainty about whether infringing materials remained
in the defendants' possession.

## Copyright-Profit Evidence

MGE sought the defendants' profits under § 504(b), but its remaining evidence
showed total company revenue from all business lines. Only a small portion of
that service business involved MGE equipment, and the software worked only with
equipment.

The court held that a copyright claimant must initially identify gross revenue
reasonably related to the infringement. Total enterprise revenue from unrelated
products and services was too broad. Because MGE supplied no sufficiently
narrowed revenue or damages evidence after its expert testimony was excluded,
the court reversed the copyright and state-law damages judgment and rendered a
take-nothing judgment on those damages claims.

That damages ruling did not erase the admitted infringement or prevent
injunctive relief. Monetary proof and entitlement to forward-looking relief were
separate issues on the reviewed record.

## Withdrawn-Opinion Boundary

The original July 2010 opinion was withdrawn when the panel granted rehearing in
part. It used materially different reasoning concerning the relationship between
circumvention and copyright infringement. The withdrawn opinion is not the
controlling Fifth Circuit decision and must not be cited as the holding of this
case.

The substituted opinion resolved the DMCA claim through absence of evidence that
the defendants themselves circumvented the measure. It did not adopt a general
rule that § 1201(a)(1) always requires proof of an independent copyright
infringement.

## Limits And Unresolved Questions

- This is Fifth Circuit authority based on an already altered software copy.
- The case did not involve proof that the accused party created or distributed
  the crack or instructed another person to perform the circumvention.
- It does not establish that knowingly obtaining or using altered software is
  lawful under copyright, contract, trade-secret, criminal, or other law.
- It does not decide § 1201(a)(2) or § 1201(b) trafficking liability for a tool,
  service, component, or instruction.
- It does not classify every hardware key, password, authentication exchange, or
  use restriction as an effective access control.
- Authorization, statutory exceptions, secondary liability, and materially
  different circuit rules require separate analysis.
- The damages holding requires a reasonable relationship between claimed gross
  revenue and infringement; it does not place the final apportionment burden on
  the claimant after that threshold is met.

## Repository Relevance

For every SHAR access-control issue, preserve the person or component that
performed each act. Acquisition, storage, execution, copying, alteration,
bypass, distribution, and publication are not interchangeable events.

Do not describe later execution of an already modified file as the circumvention
act without evidence connecting the actor to avoidance or impairment of the
measure. At the same time, do not treat absence of actor-specific DMCA proof as
permission to use, retain, publish, or distribute the file.

Never rely on the withdrawn 612 F.3d 760 opinion. Any discussion of this case
must identify the substituted 622 F.3d 361 opinion and preserve its different
reasoning.

## Required Facts

- The protected work and exact technological measure.
- The ordinary authorized process required to gain access.
- The original and altered file states and every relevant version.
- Who obtained, modified, bypassed, executed, copied, retained, or distributed
  each file.
- Logs, timestamps, devices, accounts, communications, and instructions tying an
  actor to the alleged circumvention.
- Authorization, license, contract, confidentiality, and statutory-exception
  facts.
- Every independent copyright, trade-secret, contract, and criminal-law theory.
- For damages, revenue tied to the infringing product, service, or transaction
  rather than total enterprise revenue.
- Evidence supporting continuing possession, likely recurrence, and injunctive
  relief.
- Current controlling and materially contrary authority.
