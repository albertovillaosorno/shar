# American Broadcasting Companies, Inc. v. Aereo, Inc

- Authority: 573 U.S. 431 (2014).

- As-of date: 2026-07-16.

## Question Presented

Did a subscription service publicly perform copyrighted broadcast programs when
it used subscriber-assigned antennas and subscriber-specific copies to transmit
near-live television over the Internet at each subscriber's request?

## Verified Holding And Record

The Supreme Court held that Aereo performed the broadcasters' works publicly.
The service received programs released to the public and carried them through
private channels to additional viewers using equipment housed in a centralized
facility. Its activities bore an overwhelming likeness to the cable systems that
Congress intended the 1976 transmit-clause amendments to reach.

Subscriber involvement did not change that result on the reviewed facts. Aereo's
system remained inactive until a subscriber chose a program, but the Court held
that this technological difference was not critical for a service otherwise
substantially equivalent to a cable company.

The individualized technical architecture also did not prevent a public
performance. Aereo used a separate antenna and personal copy for each subscriber
and made discrete one-to-one transmissions. The Court nevertheless held that an
entity can transmit the same performance through multiple communications and
copies to a large group of unrelated paying viewers who receive the same
contemporaneously perceptible images and sounds.

The viewers did not need to receive the performance together. The transmit
clause expressly covers recipients in different places and at different times,
and Aereo's subscribers acted as ordinary members of the public rather than as
owners or possessors of the transmitted product.

## Limited Holding And Dissent

The majority described its holding as limited. It did not decide how the
transmit clause applies to remote-storage DVRs, cloud storage, or technologies
not before the Court. It distinguished users who already own or possess relevant
content, services purchased primarily for storage rather than transmission, and
communications confined to a family or social circle.

The Court did not decide reproduction, fixation, fair use, or secondary
liability. It also did not hold that every service hosting user-selected content
performs that content. Different providers, purposes, content sources, technical
roles, and user rights require separate analysis.

Justice Scalia dissented. He would have applied a volitional-conduct rule and
treated the subscriber as the actor who selected and initiated each
transmission. He criticized the majority's cable-resemblance approach as lacking
sufficient grounding in the statutory act of performance. The majority's cable-
equivalent public-performance holding controls. The dissent identifies an
unresolved attribution concern for materially different technologies.

## Relationship To Cartoon Network

*Cartoon Network LP, LLLP v. CSC Holdings, Inc.* held that transmissions from a
subscriber's unique remote-DVR copy to that same subscriber were not public
performances on its record. *Aereo* later rejected reliance on individualized
antennas, copies, and transmissions for a cable-like service delivering
broadcast programming to many unrelated paying subscribers.

*Aereo* did not decide the short-lived buffer fixation issue or declare every
remote-DVR transmission public. The exact service, purpose, audience, source
copy, user rights, and functional resemblance to regulated transmitters remain
necessary.

## Repository Relevance

A streaming or retransmission design cannot avoid public-performance analysis
merely by assigning a unique copy, connection, process, or server object to each
viewer. User selection and one-to-one delivery are relevant facts, not automatic
safe harbors, when the service functions like a content transmitter to the
public.

SHAR must separately analyze local playback, creator-uploaded gameplay, remote
storage, multiplayer spectators, dedicated servers, live broadcasts, and any
service that selects or retransmits protected audiovisual content. Public
performance does not itself decide reproduction, fair use, publisher permission,
or platform eligibility.

## Required Facts

- The work, broadcast, performance, source signal, and source copy.
- The provider's equipment, facilities, antennas, servers, and storage.
- Who selects the content and initiates each transmission.
- Whether the service resembles a cable or broadcast retransmitter.
- The number, relationship, and legal status of potential recipients.
- Whether users own or possess the content or pay primarily for storage.
- Whether transmissions communicate contemporaneously perceptible images and
  sounds.
- Reproduction, fixation, fair-use, licensing, and secondary-liability issues
  analyzed separately.
- Later controlling authority and the governing jurisdiction.
