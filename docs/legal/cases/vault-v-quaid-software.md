# Vault Corp. v. Quaid Software Ltd

> [Legal Research Disclaimer](../disclaimer.md) applies to this record.

- Status: Reported Fifth Circuit opinion text verified from a permitted
  secondary duplicate; an official archival PDF was not located.
- Court: United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit.
- Authority: 847 F.2d 255 (5th Cir. 1988).
- Decision date: 1988-06-20.
- As-of date: 2026-07-15.
- Counsel review: Not performed.

## Question Presented

Did loading a computer program into memory for analysis infringe copyright, and
could Louisiana law make a license restriction against decompilation or
disassembly enforceable despite 17 U.S.C. § 117?

## Verified Holding And Record

The court held that the program owner's loading of the software into memory for
analysis fell within the essential-step rule then codified in § 117(1). The
statute did not limit the permitted copy to a purpose intended by the copyright
owner, and the court declined to add that restriction.

The court also held that the challenged copy-enabling feature was capable of a
substantial noninfringing use because it enabled owners of copy-protected
programs to make fully functional archival copies. It rejected the contributory
infringement claim and held that the reviewed program was not a substantially
similar derivative work.

Louisiana's Software License Enforcement Act permitted licensors to prohibit
copying, modification, reverse engineering, decompilation, and disassembly. The
court held that the decompilation-or-disassembly provision conflicted with the
rights of computer-program owners under § 117 and was preempted by federal law.
The corresponding restriction in the reviewed license was therefore
unenforceable.

## Limits And Later Distinction

- The reviewed license depended on a Louisiana statute that authorized specified
  terms. The district court had treated the agreement as an adhesion contract
  enforceable only if that statute was valid.
- The opinion did not establish that every privately negotiated or mass-market
  promise restricting reverse engineering is preempted.
- *Bowers v. Baystate Technologies, Inc.* later distinguished *Vault* while
  enforcing a private contractual restriction under the First Circuit law it
  predicted would apply.
- The decision predates the Digital Millennium Copyright Act. It does not
  resolve current § 1201 access-control, trafficking, or exemption questions.
- Section 117 does not authorize retention, transfer, sale, or distribution
  outside its statutory conditions.
- The court's archival-copy and contributory-infringement analysis depended on
  the record and the statutory text then before it.

## Repository Relevance

The case supports a narrow distinction between federal rights of a qualifying
program-copy owner and state rules that attempt to eliminate those rights. It
also demonstrates that local loading or archival copying can differ legally from
redistribution.

SHAR cannot infer from this decision that every operator owns the relevant
program copy, accepted no enforceable agreement, may bypass an access control,
or may share game files. Transaction evidence, current statutes, governing law,
and the exact act remain necessary.
