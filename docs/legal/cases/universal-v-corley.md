# Universal City Studios, Inc. v. Corley

> [Legal Research Disclaimer](../disclaimer.md) applies to this record.

- Status: Reported Second Circuit opinion text verified from a public-domain
  reporter duplicate; an official archival court copy was not located.
- Court: United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit.
- Authority: 273 F.3d 429 (2d Cir. 2001).
- Docket: 00-9185.
- Decision date: 2001-11-28.
- As-of date: 2026-07-16.
- Counsel review: Not performed.

## Question Presented

Could the Digital Millennium Copyright Act prohibit posting and purposefully
linking to software that decrypted the access control on encrypted DVDs, and did
the injunction violate fair-use or First Amendment protections?

## Verified Holding And Record

The Second Circuit affirmed the injunction against posting the DeCSS decryption
program and against the challenged linking conduct. The program decrypted the
Content Scramble System used to control access to copyrighted motion pictures on
DVDs. On the reviewed record, distributing the program satisfied the
§ 1201(a)(2) anti-trafficking prohibition.

The court treated computer code as protected expression but also as technology
with functional capability. It applied intermediate scrutiny to the content-
neutral regulation of that functional capability and held the statute and
injunction valid as applied. The injunction regulated dissemination of the
circumvention functionality rather than discussion of the underlying ideas.

The court rejected the argument that potential fair uses of decrypted motion
pictures created a right to distribute the decryption tool. The appellants were
not barred from making fair use of copyrighted material; they were barred from
trafficking in technology that enabled unauthorized access. The opinion did not
recognize a general fair-use defense to the anti-trafficking claim.

The affirmed linking restriction was record-specific. The district court had
required clear and convincing evidence that the linker knew the prohibited
material was on the destination site, knew it was unlawfully offered
circumvention technology, and created or maintained the link to disseminate that
technology. The Second Circuit upheld the injunction as applied without deciding
that this exact test is required in every linking case.

## Limits And Later Context

- The opinion involved encrypted commercial motion pictures, a decryption
  program, widespread online distribution, and purposeful links to copies of the
  same tool.
- It did not hold that every program, source-code publication, hyperlink,
  security discussion, compatibility tool, or encryption research project is
  prohibited.
- The court recognized code as expression; the outcome depended on the tool's
  functional circumvention capacity and the statutory and evidentiary record.
- The linking result did not impose strict liability for every link to a site
  that later contains prohibited material.
- Statutory exceptions remain available only when every condition is met. A
  general Linux-compatibility or research objective did not establish an
  exception on the reviewed record.
- Later cases including *Chamberlain Group, Inc. v. Skylink Technologies, Inc.*,
  *Lexmark International, Inc. v. Static Control Components, Inc.*, and *MDY
  Industries, LLC v. Blizzard Entertainment, Inc.* apply materially different
  access-control facts and circuit rules.
- The decision does not determine the legality of a particular SHAR parser,
  publication, hyperlink, or independently created compatible implementation.

## Repository Relevance

SHAR must never publish or link to a tool, payload, key, or instruction intended
to defeat an effective access control merely because a downstream user might
make a lawful use of the resulting material. Tool distribution, access,
infringement, fair use, and statutory exceptions are separate analyses.

Technical discussion can explain lawful repository behavior without publishing
functional circumvention material. Any external link involving access-control
technology requires verification of the destination, purpose, offered material,
current status, and publication authority. Direct links to proprietary payloads
or prohibited circumvention material remain outside the repository boundary.

## Required Facts

- The protected work, encryption or access-control measure, and ordinary access
  route.
- The exact code, tool, service, instruction, or linked material.
- Functional capabilities, design, documentation, marketing, and actual uses.
- Every statutory exception asserted and evidence for each condition.
- The publisher's knowledge, purpose, recipients, and distribution method.
- For links, destination content, knowledge at the relevant time, link purpose,
  and later changes.
- The speech interest, functional regulation, scope of relief, and controlling
  First Amendment authority.
- Governing circuit law and later treatment.
