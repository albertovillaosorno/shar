## Question Presented

What notice and manifestation of assent were sufficient for the online terms at
issue, and how has later Second Circuit law treated that decision?

## Verified Holding

The district court held, without appeal on the point, that California law
governs contract formation. The Second Circuit agreed that "a reasonably prudent
Internet user in circumstances such as these would not have known or learned of
the existence of the license terms before responding to defendants' invitation
to download the free software," because the reference to the SmartDownload
license terms was located on a screen below the download button. Downloading
alone therefore did not manifest assent, and the court rejected the argument
that placement on the next scrollable screen put users on inquiry notice.

The court affirmed the denial of the motion to compel arbitration and to stay
proceedings: the user plaintiffs were not bound by the arbitration clause in the
SmartDownload terms, their claims were not arbitrable under the separate
Communicator license agreement, and plaintiff Specht could not be compelled to
arbitrate as a nonparty who had not received a direct benefit under a contract
containing the arbitration agreement.

## Verified Later Authorities

Later Ninth Circuit opinions describe *Specht* as involving terms presented
below a download button or on a submerged screen and use it when discussing
notice and manifestation of assent:

- [Nguyen v. Barnes & Noble, Inc.](nguyen-v-barnes-and-noble.md).
- [Berman v. Freedom Financial Network, LLC](berman-v-freedom-financial.md).

## Not Established

- That every browsewrap agreement is unenforceable.
- That a hyperlink below the visible screen always defeats assent.
- That clickwrap or sign-in-wrap is automatically enforceable.
- That absence of a signed NDA eliminates every contractual duty.
- That copyright or DMCA rights automatically override an agreed contract.
- That the current Second Circuit standard is fully stated by this opinion
  rather than by later controlling decisions.
