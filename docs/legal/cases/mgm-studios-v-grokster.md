## Question Presented

Can a distributor be secondarily liable when it promotes software for copyright
infringement and takes affirmative steps to foster that use, even if the
software is capable of substantial noninfringing uses?

## Verified Holding And Record

The Supreme Court held that a distributor who provides a device with the object
of promoting copyright infringement can be liable for resulting third-party
infringement. The unlawful objective must be shown through clear expression or
other affirmative steps that foster infringement and go beyond ordinary product
distribution with knowledge of user conduct.

The rule applied regardless of whether the peer-to-peer software was capable of
substantial noninfringing uses. *Sony Corp. of America v. Universal City
Studios, Inc.* limited secondary liability based only on product distribution
and knowledge that some users might infringe. It did not protect active
inducement.

The reviewed evidence could support inducement because the distributors sought
users of a predecessor file-sharing service known for infringement, promoted
their software as a replacement, relied on advertising revenue that increased
with user volume, and gave no evidence of attempts to filter copyrighted
files.
The absence of filtering was not independently sufficient but could reinforce
other evidence of unlawful purpose.

## Evidence Boundary

The Court required purposeful, culpable expression and conduct. Mere knowledge
of infringing potential or actual infringing uses is not enough. Ordinary acts
incident to product distribution, including technical support and product
updates, do not support liability by themselves when no other evidence shows an
intent to promote infringement.

A court must also establish direct infringement by third parties and a causal
relationship between the inducement and resulting infringement. Relevant
evidence can include the distributor's business model, target audience, internal
communications, public statements, product design, documentation, and support
practices.

## Relationship To Sony

*Sony* protects ordinary distribution of a product capable of substantial
noninfringing uses from secondary liability based solely on the product's
capability and constructive knowledge. *Grokster* addresses additional evidence
that the distributor intended and encouraged infringement.

The cases do not create opposing universal presumptions. A product with lawful
uses can still be distributed with an unlawful objective, while a product used
for infringement is not automatically induced by its distributor. The exact
communications and affirmative conduct control.

## Limits And Unresolved Questions

- The holding concerns inducement, not every contributory or vicarious theory.
- Product capability remains relevant but does not override proven inducement.
- Knowledge, popularity among infringers, or failure to filter is not sufficient
  alone.
- Technical support, updates, neutral compatibility information, and ordinary
  documentation are not automatically inducement.
- Marketing directed at infringing demand, instructions that facilitate
  infringement, and business plans dependent on infringement can support intent.
- The Court did not establish a general duty to monitor or filter all users.
- Direct infringement, causation, remedies, and every defendant's conduct remain
  fact-specific.
- Contract, access-control, trademark, patent, and platform-policy issues
  require separate analysis.

## Repository Relevance

SHAR's public wording, examples, feature names, tutorials, demonstrations, and
support messages must promote lawful interoperability and
independently authored use. The repository must not invite piracy, advertise
access to unauthorized content, target users seeking infringing substitutes, or
provide operational support for unlawful distribution.

Neutral technical capability is not enough when surrounding evidence
communicates an unlawful objective. Conversely, a known risk does not justify
misstating a lawful tool as inducement without evidence of purposeful promotion
or affirmative steps.

## Required Facts

- The product, service, functions, lawful uses, and infringing uses.
- Public and private statements about intended users and purposes.
- Marketing channels, target audiences, examples, tutorials, and demonstrations.
- Product design, defaults, filtering choices, updates, and technical support.
- Revenue sources and whether infringement drives the business model.
- Evidence of direct infringement and causation from alleged inducement.
- Later authority, jurisdiction, and every separate liability theory.
