# Vernor v. Autodesk, Inc

- Court: United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit.
- Authority: 621 F.3d 1102 (9th Cir. 2010); No. 09-35969.
- Decision date: 2010-09-10.
- As-of date: 2026-07-13.
- Counsel review: Not performed.

## Question Presented

When does the Ninth Circuit treat a software transferee as a licensee rather
than an owner of a copy for the first-sale doctrine and the essential-step
defense?

## Verified Holding And Record

The court held that a software user is a licensee rather than an owner of a copy
when the copyright owner specifies that the user receives a license,
significantly restricts transfer, and imposes notable use restrictions. It
applied those considerations to an agreement that reserved title, prohibited or
conditioned transfer, imposed substantial use restrictions, and allowed
termination for noncompliance.

Because the direct customer was a licensee, the court held that the customer
could not rely on 17 U.S.C. § 109(a) to resell the copies. The downstream buyer
and its customers also could not invoke 17 U.S.C. § 117(a)(1) for installation
copies because they had not obtained ownership of the relevant program copies.
The court therefore vacated the copyright judgment for the downstream reseller
and remanded the remaining issues.

The court distinguished [*Krause v. Titleserv, Inc.*](krause-v-titleserv.md)
because that record had no written license agreement, the user had paid
substantial consideration for programs developed for its sole benefit, and the
developer had allowed continued use without a termination date. The distinction
confirms that ownership is a fact- and agreement-specific inquiry, not a
conclusion supplied by the physical medium or by the word `license` alone.

## Limits And Procedural Boundary

- The three-consideration rule is Ninth Circuit authority and does not erase
  materially different ownership tests in other jurisdictions.
- The decision concerns ownership of particular software copies for §§ 109 and
  117; it does not decide every contract, fair-use, or anti-circumvention issue.
- A receipt, disc, one-time payment, or indefinite possession is relevant
  evidence but does not independently establish ownership under this rule.
- The exact transfer documents, retained-title language, restrictions, assent,
  chain of title, and governing jurisdiction remain necessary facts.

## Repository Relevance

SHAR must not infer § 117 ownership merely from possession of a physical game
copy. A copy-specific review must preserve the purchase and chain-of-title
evidence together with packaging, license text, installation terms, assent
facts, transfer restrictions, use restrictions, and governing jurisdiction.
That inquiry remains separate from fair use, interoperability, contract, and
Section 1201 analysis.
