# Authors Guild v. Google, Inc

## Question Presented

Did unauthorized digitization of complete books, creation of a full-text search
index, limited display of responsive snippets, and delivery of digital copies to
source libraries qualify as fair use?

## Verified Holding And Record

Google made and retained digital copies of millions of library books, including
copyrighted books, to create a searchable database. A public user could learn
whether and where a search term appeared and could see limited snippets around
responsive terms. The public could not read complete copyrighted books through
the service.

The Second Circuit held that digitization for the search function was highly
transformative because it provided information about the books rather than a
substitute reading experience. The search output served a different purpose from
the expressive purpose of the books and expanded their utility for research,
discovery, and linguistic analysis.

Google's commercial status and profit motivation did not defeat fair use on that
record. Commerciality remained relevant, but the strongly different purpose and
restricted public output carried greater weight under the first factor.

## Complete Copying And Snippet Limits

Copying each complete book was reasonable in relation to the search purpose.
Full-text processing was necessary to identify every responsive location and to
support the index. The amount used internally therefore did not make the third
factor weigh against fair use by itself.

The public display was materially narrower than the internal copy. Snippet view
showed small segments around search terms and incorporated restrictions designed
to prevent users from retrieving a continuous or substantial portion of a book.
The court assessed the complete system, including those output limits, rather
than treating internal possession of a full copy as equivalent to public access.

The decision does not establish that every snippet length, preview design, or
anti-reconstruction control is sufficient. The quantity, sequence, query limits,
work type, excluded pages, and ability to reconstruct protected expression
remain necessary facts.

## Market Analysis

The search and snippet functions did not provide a significant substitute for
the protected expression in the books. The court rejected a claimed licensing
market for the same informational search function because copyright owners do
not receive an exclusive derivative right to provide information about their
works.

The court recognized that snippets might sometimes satisfy a user's need for a
small fact and could theoretically affect a sale. On the reviewed record, those
limited effects did not make the service a meaningful substitute for the books
or their protected derivatives.

## Library Copies And Security Risk

Google allowed participating libraries to download copies of books they had
submitted. The agreements required lawful use, and the record did not establish
that the libraries were using the copies unlawfully. Supplying those copies was
therefore not infringement on the reviewed facts, and speculative concern that a
library might later misuse or inadequately secure a copy did not establish
contributory infringement.

The court did not hold that security risk is irrelevant. It concluded only that
the record did not show an unreasonable risk of loss of copyright value through
hacking. Different access controls, recipients, contractual duties, incidents,
or evidence of leakage could produce a different analysis.

## Later Limits

The Supreme Court denied certiorari without addressing the merits. A denial of
certiorari is not approval of the lower court's reasoning.

<!-- markdownlint-disable-next-line MD013 -->
[*Andy Warhol Foundation for the Visual Arts, Inc. v. Goldsmith*](andy-warhol-foundation-v-goldsmith.md)
requires the first factor to examine the specific challenged use, its purpose,
and commercial context. A claimed new purpose does not replace analysis of all
four factors.

<!-- markdownlint-disable-next-line MD013 -->
[*Hachette Book Group, Inc. v. Internet Archive*](hachette-book-group-v-internet-archive.md)
later distinguished Google Books. The Second Circuit rejected fair use where
complete digital books were made available for users to read for free. That
service served the same purpose as the original books and substituted for
authorized print and electronic-book markets, even when lending was ordinarily
limited by a one-copy-to-one-user ratio.

## Limits And Unresolved Questions

- This is Second Circuit authority based on the particular Google Books design.
- It does not create a general exception for artificial-intelligence training,
  data mining, archives, preservation, search engines, or internal repositories.
- It does not authorize public access to complete works or reconstructable
  protected expression.
- It does not establish that commercial indexing is always transformative.
- It does not excuse copying when a less extensive method can serve the actual
  purpose.
- It does not resolve contract, access-control, privacy, confidentiality,
  database, trade-secret, or platform restrictions.
- Unpublished works, different media, source code, audiovisual assets, music,
  and highly functional works can present materially different factor analyses.
- Current controlling authority and contrary decisions remain necessary.

## Repository Relevance

SHAR may need complete local input to classify, search, compare, or generate
metadata that does not reproduce protected expression. *Authors Guild* supports
separate analysis of internal technical copying and a constrained public output.
It does not approve the SHAR workflow automatically.

A public index should disclose only what the technical purpose requires. It must
not contain reconstructable game code, dialogue, music, textures, models,
protected screenshots, long excerpts, or a substitute for the original files.
Derived identifiers, independently authored descriptions, aggregate statistics,
and minimal metadata that does not reproduce expression generally present a
narrower publication surface than source material or expressive previews.

Every intermediate full copy remains a separate act requiring a lawful basis.
Preserve the purpose, necessity, retention, access controls, and output
restrictions, plus security evidence, alternatives, and market analysis. Keep
protected local input
outside public history unless artifact-specific distribution authority exists.

## Required Facts

- Every source work, rights holder, publication status, and acquisition basis.
- The exact reason complete copying is technically necessary.
- Every intermediate and retained copy, location, recipient, and retention time.
- The search index, query behavior, result format, excerpt size, and rate
  limits.
- Whether repeated queries can reconstruct protected expression.
- Access controls, security testing, incidents, and contractual safeguards.
- The public and commercial purpose of each output.
- Existing and likely markets for the original, derivatives, search, and data.
- Less extensive technical alternatives and why they were inadequate.
- Current controlling and materially contrary authority.
