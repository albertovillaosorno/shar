# Adobe Systems, Inc. v. Christenson

> [Legal Research Disclaimer](../disclaimer.md) applies to this record.

- Status: Published Ninth Circuit opinion verified from a public-domain legal
  duplicate; current Ninth Circuit model instructions continue to cite its
  first-sale burden rule.
- Court: United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit.
- Authority: 809 F.3d 1071 (9th Cir. 2015).
- Docket: 12-17371.
- Decision date: 2015-12-30.
- Disposition: Summary judgment for the downstream reseller affirmed.
- As-of date: 2026-07-16.
- Counsel review: Not performed.

## Question Presented

Who bears the evidentiary burdens when a downstream software reseller invokes
§ 109(a) and the copyright owner responds that the genuine copies were licensed
rather than sold?

## Verified Copyright Holding

Adobe established registered copyrights, identified matching software titles,
and showed unauthorized downstream sales. That evidence established a prima
facie infringement claim against the reseller.

The reseller nevertheless carried the initial burden for the first-sale defense.
At summary judgment, the proponent must produce evidence sufficient for a jury
to find lawful acquisition of ownership through purchase or another transaction
in genuine copies. Possession alone is not enough.

The reseller produced supplier invoices and a sworn account of its purchases.
The court treated that evidence as sufficient on the reviewed record. Once that
initial showing was made, the burden shifted back to Adobe because Adobe claimed
that the original transfers were licenses rather than sales.

The copyright owner was required to establish the asserted license or absence of
an ownership-transferring sale. That allocation reflected the owner's superior
access to its original agreements and avoided requiring a remote downstream
holder to prove the nonexistence or unknown terms of an earlier license.

## Actual Agreement Evidence

Adobe did not produce admissible agreements for the particular copies and
transactions. General testimony that Adobe ordinarily licensed software and
generic license templates did not establish the actual transfer terms under
[*Vernor v. Autodesk, Inc.*](vernor-v-autodesk.md). The agreement title was not
dispositive; its precise restrictions and legal effect mattered.

The procedural record was material. The district court excluded late-offered
license evidence after discovery disputes, and the Ninth Circuit found no abuse
of discretion. The opinion does not establish that general testimony or a
standard form can never support a license issue. It establishes that a claimant
must produce admissible evidence sufficient to prove the operative transaction.

## Trademark Holding

The court also affirmed judgment on Adobe's trademark-infringement claim under
Ninth Circuit nominative-use doctrine. The reseller used Adobe marks to identify
the genuine products themselves rather than to suggest sponsorship or
endorsement on the reviewed presentation.

Adobe's attempted false-advertising theory was not decided on the merits because
it had not been pleaded. The trademark holding does not authorize confusing
branding, logos, false compatibility claims, passing off, or materially
different products.

## Limits And Unresolved Questions

- This is Ninth Circuit authority governing a particular evidentiary record.
- The first-sale proponent must still show lawful acquisition of genuine copies.
- Invoices or sworn testimony are not automatically sufficient in every case.
- Admissible copy-specific license terms can establish that no sale occurred.
- The case does not authorize reproduction, installation, adaptation,
  circumvention, retention of duplicates, or transfer of copyright ownership.
- Counterfeit, unauthorized, altered, or unlawfully made copies remain outside
  the established holding.
- Other circuits may allocate burdens or determine copy ownership differently.
- Contract formation, assent, downstream restrictions, and consumer claims
  require separate analysis.

## Repository Relevance

SHAR must preserve copy provenance separately from copyright ownership. A
receipt or invoice can support lawful downstream acquisition, but it does not
prove that the original copyright owner sold rather than licensed the copy.

When a license is asserted, preserve the actual agreement for the particular
version, channel, bundle, account, medium, device, and original recipient. Do
not replace missing transactional evidence with a generic current template,
product page, customary-practice statement, or the word `license` on packaging.

Compatibility naming should identify the genuine target accurately while
keeping SHAR's own identity prominent and avoiding any suggestion of
sponsorship, endorsement, approval, or official support.

## Required Facts

- The exact copy, version, medium, serial or entitlement, and acquisition date.
- Supplier invoices, receipts, payment records, packaging, and transfer history.
- Evidence that each copy is genuine and was lawfully made and acquired.
- The original transfer agreement and every material downstream restriction.
- Whether title passed under controlling ownership law.
- Every retained, installed, reproduced, adapted, or transferred copy.
- The exact trademark wording, product condition, presentation, and disclaimer.
- The governing circuit and materially contrary authority.
