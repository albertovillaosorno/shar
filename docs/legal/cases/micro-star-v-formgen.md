# Micro Star v. FormGen, Inc

> [Legal Research Disclaimer](../disclaimer.md) applies to this record.

- Status: Reported Ninth Circuit opinion text verified from an archived
  duplicate.
- Court: United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit.
- Authority: 154 F.3d 1107 (9th Cir. 1998); Nos. 96-56426 and 96-56433.
- Decision date: 1998-09-11.
- As-of date: 2026-07-13.
- Counsel review: Not performed.

## Question Presented

Did commercially distributed user-created level-description files constitute
unauthorized derivative works, and did fair use or an implied license protect
the distributor?

## Verified Holding And Record

The court held that the game publisher was likely to succeed on its claim that a
commercial CD containing user-created level files infringed the publisher's
exclusive derivative-work right. The files permanently described new audiovisual
levels in exact detail and, when used with the original game, drew the resulting
expression from the game's protected art, setting, characters, and story.

The court distinguished temporary runtime changes in *Lewis Galoob Toys v.
Nintendo*. The distributed level files existed in a concrete or permanent form
and described the challenged displays rather than merely changing a transient
value while the original game ran.

The court rejected fair use on the preliminary-injunction record because the
use was commercial, drew substantially from a highly creative fictional work,
was not meaningfully transformative, and entered the copyright owner's potential
market for new versions and sequels. It also held that encouragement to create
and share levels did not authorize the commercial compilation: the identified
player license limited distribution to free sharing.

## Limits And Procedural Boundary

- The appeal reviewed preliminary-injunction relief and the copyright owner's
  likelihood of success, not a final damages judgment after trial.
- The result depended on permanently distributed files that described new game
  displays and stories, commercial exploitation, substantial use of protected
  expression, and an express noncommercial license limitation.
- The decision does not establish that every configuration, map, patch, or
  independently authored compatibility file is a derivative work.
- The court characterized *Galoob*'s alternative fair-use discussion as dicta;
  that later treatment must accompany any reliance on *Galoob*.
- Permission to create or share user content is not permission to sell it or to
  use protected expression outside the exact license scope.

## Repository Relevance

SHAR must keep fixed public packages materially different from transient local
runtime changes. A distributable file can present derivative-work risk even when
it contains instructions rather than embedded art if it permanently describes
protected audiovisual expression and depends on the original work to render it.
No publisher tool, invitation to mod, or free-sharing practice may be expanded
into commercial or public rights beyond its exact terms.
