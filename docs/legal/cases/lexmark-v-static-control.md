# Lexmark International, Inc. v. Static Control Components, Inc

> [Legal Research Disclaimer](../disclaimer.md) applies to this record.

- Status: Primary Sixth Circuit opinion verified.
- Court: United States Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit.
- Authority: 387 F.3d 522 (6th Cir. 2004).
- Decision date: 2004-10-26.
- As-of date: 2026-07-16.
- Counsel review: Not performed.

## Question Presented

How did the Sixth Circuit assess copyright and DMCA claims involving printer
software, an authentication sequence, and replacement-cartridge compatibility?

## Verified Holding And Record

The Sixth Circuit vacated the preliminary injunction because Lexmark had not
shown the required likelihood of success on the addressed copyright and DMCA
claims.

For the Printer Engine Program, the court rejected the theory that the
authentication sequence effectively controlled access to the program. A person
who purchased the printer could read the program's literal code from printer
memory without using or defeating the authentication sequence. The sequence
restricted one form of use of the printer; on those facts it did not block
access to an otherwise unavailable copyrighted work.

The opinion also analyzed the Toner Loading Program through copyrightability,
merger, and scenes-a-faire principles. It did not treat the mere theoretical
availability of alternative implementations as conclusive proof of protectable
expression.

Like *Chamberlain Group, Inc. v. Skylink Technologies, Inc.*, the decision
requires an exact account of the work, measure, ordinary access route, and
accused compatibility technology. The cases reach their results through
different statutory failures: *Lexmark* found no effective control over access
to otherwise available program code, while *Chamberlain* found authorization and
no required access-to-infringement nexus in the Federal Circuit.

## Limits And Procedural Posture

- The case reviewed a preliminary injunction, not a final merits judgment after
  a complete trial.
- The opinion does not hold that an authentication or compatibility control can
  never qualify under the DMCA.
- It does not create a universal anti-monopoly or aftermarket exception.
- It does not authorize copying source code, firmware, or protected content.
- The panel opinions and later treatment must be separated carefully.
- Later references to abrogation concern unrelated injunction standards, not a
  wholesale overruling of the software and access-control analysis.

## Repository Relevance

The case requires a precise technical account of the alleged protected work, the
measure, the route by which the work is otherwise accessible, and the accused
tool's function. A label such as authentication, package validation, or
compatibility lock does not decide the statutory elements.

## Open Questions

- Does any SHAR workflow encounter a measure that actually controls access to a
  protected work rather than only controlling use or compatibility?
- Is the relevant expression constrained by standards, compatibility,
  efficiency, or industry practice?
- What later authority controls in the jurisdiction of any dispute?
