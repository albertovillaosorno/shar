# Hachette Book Group, Inc. v. Internet Archive

> [Legal Research Disclaimer](../disclaimer.md) applies to this record.

- Status: Published Second Circuit opinion verified from a public-domain opinion
  duplicate; later procedural history verified from the announced final
  resolution of the litigation.
- Court: United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit.
- Authority: 115 F.4th 163 (2d Cir. 2024).
- Docket: 23-1260.
- Decision date: 2024-09-04.
- Disposition: Summary judgment for the publishers affirmed.
- Later history: Internet Archive declined to seek Supreme Court review by the
  2024-12-03 filing deadline.
- As-of date: 2026-07-16.
- Counsel review: Not performed.

## Question Presented

Is it fair use for a nonprofit organization to scan complete copyrighted print
books and distribute full digital copies online for free when the service
ordinarily limits simultaneous loans to the number of corresponding physical
copies held by it or participating libraries?

## Verified Holding And Record

Internet Archive scanned print books and made complete digital copies available
to account holders through its Free Digital Library. Except during the National
Emergency Library period, it ordinarily allowed one simultaneous digital loan
for each physical copy counted in its own or a participating library's
collection. The physical books used to support those loan counts were kept out
of ordinary circulation.

The publishers alleged infringement of 127 fiction and nonfiction books. Each
work was available in an authorized electronic format for consumer purchase or
library licensing. Internet Archive and its users lacked permission to reproduce
or distribute the challenged digital copies.

The Second Circuit held that the challenged practices were not fair use. A
one-to-one physical-to-digital loan ratio did not authorize creation of a new
digital copy or make public distribution of the complete copy fair. The court
affirmed the judgment and declined to narrow the injunction to digital lending
based only on physical books that Internet Archive itself owned.

## Purpose And Commerciality

The digital books served the same purpose as the originals: allowing users to
read the protected works. Converting a print book into a digital format did not
add criticism, commentary, information about the work, or another sufficiently
different purpose. Convenience, remote delivery, link insertion, and a change of
format did not make the use transformative on the reviewed record.

The Second Circuit disagreed with the district court's characterization of the
use as commercial. Internet Archive was a nonprofit, charged no fee for the
service, and received no sufficiently direct profit from donations or linked
used-book purchases. That noncommercial character favored Internet Archive but
did not overcome the absence of transformation or the other factors.

## Nature And Amount

The works included published fiction and nonfiction. Although some books
conveyed factual information, the authors' selection, arrangement, and
expression remained protected. The second factor favored the publishers.

Internet Archive copied and revealed the complete books to the public. Complete
copying can be reasonable when necessary for a different, transformative
purpose, but the digital copies here served as reading substitutes. The third
factor therefore favored the publishers.

## Market Analysis

The publishers maintained established markets for consumer electronic books and
library electronic-book licenses. Internet Archive made complete substitutes
available without paying the customary licensing price and promoted the service
as a way for libraries to avoid purchasing the same content in another format.

The court accepted a logical inference of market harm even though the
publishers did not supply conclusive empirical evidence of lost sales.
Widespread conduct of the same kind would give consumers and libraries less
reason to pay for permission to obtain the same books digitally. The fourth
factor favored the publishers.

The result did not depend on the publishers proving losses from each individual
loan. The fair-use proponent bore the burden of showing that the secondary use
did not compete in the relevant markets, and Internet Archive's evidence did not
carry that burden.

## First Sale And Copy-Control Boundary

Section 109 permits the owner of a particular physical copy to sell, lend, or
otherwise dispose of that copy. It does not authorize reproduction of the work
into a different material object. Retaining one physical copy for each digital
loan did not cancel the reproduction and public-distribution acts involved in
creating and lending the digital copy.

The case follows the same material-object boundary identified in
[*Capitol Records, LLC v. ReDigi Inc.*](capitol-records-v-redigi.md). A policy
that limits simultaneous access, destroys another instance, or removes an
original from circulation does not independently authorize a new copy.

## Relationship To Search And Indexing

The court distinguished
[*Authors Guild v. Google, Inc.*](authors-guild-v-google.md). Google processed
complete books internally to provide search information and restricted snippets;
it did not reveal complete digital books to users. Internet Archive instead made
the full protected expression available for the original reading purpose.

That distinction separates a constrained search or indexing output from a
service that distributes complete substitutes. The amount copied internally,
the material exposed publicly, the user's purpose, the ability to reconstruct
protected expression, and the relevant markets must all be evaluated.

## Limits And Unresolved Questions

- This is Second Circuit authority based on complete digital copies of published
  books made available for reading.
- The court found the use noncommercial; nonprofit status still did not
  establish fair use.
- The decision does not create a categorical rule for every library,
  preservation, accessibility, research, or archival workflow.
- It does not decide whether a specific activity qualifies under 17 U.S.C. § 108
  or another statutory exception.
- It does not decide public-domain works, licensed works, unavailable works, or
  outputs that contain no protected expression.
- It does not establish the result for artificial-intelligence training, source
  code analysis, local reverse engineering, or materially different media.
- Physical ownership, copy count, access limits, and noncommercial purpose
  remain relevant facts, but none is independently dispositive.
- Current controlling authority and materially contrary decisions remain
  necessary.

## Repository Relevance

Owning a game disc or another physical copy does not authorize SHAR to create a
public digital substitute. Removing the physical item from circulation, limiting
concurrent downloads, encrypting a copy, or deleting access after a fixed period
does not eliminate the reproduction or distribution analysis.

Complete local input used for lawful technical analysis must remain separate
from every public output. Repository manifests, indexes, documentation,
fixtures, and previews must not expose reconstructable game code, assets,
dialogue, music, models, textures, or another substitute for the original files.

Search metadata and independently authored technical descriptions can present a
materially narrower use than full-copy distribution. That narrower design still
requires purpose, necessity, output, security, market, contract, and
jurisdiction-specific review.

## Required Facts

- Every physical and digital copy, owner, location, and acquisition basis.
- The precise reproduction, format conversion, encryption, and delivery steps.
- The number of physical copies and simultaneous digital instances.
- Whether the physical copies remain usable, circulate, or are withheld.
- The exact content available to users and whether it substitutes for the work.
- Access controls, loan duration, recipients, territory, and leakage history.
- Available consumer, library, platform, and derivative licensing markets.
- The purpose and commercial or nonprofit context of each challenged use.
- Applicable licenses, statutory exceptions, and controlling jurisdiction.
