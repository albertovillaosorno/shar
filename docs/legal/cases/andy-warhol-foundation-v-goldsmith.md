# Andy Warhol Foundation for the Visual Arts, Inc. v. Goldsmith

- Status: Primary Supreme Court opinion verified.
- Court: Supreme Court of the United States.
- Authority: 598 U.S. 508 (2023).
- Decision date: 2023-05-18.
- As-of date: 2026-07-15.
- Counsel review: Not performed.

## Question Presented

What did the Supreme Court decide about the first fair-use factor when the Andy
Warhol Foundation commercially licensed an image derived from Lynn Goldsmith's
photograph for a magazine cover?

## Verified Holding And Record

The Court reviewed only the Foundation's 2016 commercial license of the
`Orange Prince` image to Condé Nast. It did not decide whether creating,
displaying, or selling the original Prince Series works was fair use.

The Court held that the first fair-use factor did not favor the Foundation. The
challenged image and Goldsmith's photograph served substantially the same
purpose: each was a portrait of Prince used to illustrate a magazine story about
Prince. The Foundation's use was also commercial.

New expression, meaning, or message may remain relevant, but it is not
independently dispositive. The first factor asks whether and to what extent the
specific challenged use has a further purpose or different character. That
difference is a matter of degree and must be weighed with commerciality and the
copyright owner's derivative-work right.

The Court explained that when the original and challenged use share the same or
a highly similar purpose and the challenged use is commercial, the first factor
is likely to weigh against fair use unless another justification supports the
copying. Commerciality is relevant but does not decide the factor by itself.

## Limits And Contrary Reasoning

- The Court decided only the first statutory factor and only for the challenged
  commercial license.
- The opinion did not determine whether every Prince Series work or every use of
  one was infringing or fair.
- The decision does not eliminate fair use for commentary, criticism, parody,
  research, interoperability, or other sufficiently distinct purposes.
- A different message or visual treatment does not automatically establish a
  different legal purpose.
- Justice Kagan, joined by Chief Justice Roberts, dissented and would have given
  greater weight to the new expression and meaning of Warhol's work.

## Repository Relevance

SHAR must assess the exact act and output at issue rather than describing an
entire project, video, or publication as transformative. For gameplay videos,
commentary or criticism tied to the footage may present a different purpose from
unedited entertainment footage, but monetization, amount used, market effects,
and the remaining statutory factors still require separate analysis.

The decision does not authorize copying game assets, distributing modified game
files, bypassing access controls, or treating a platform monetization decision
as a legal ruling.

## Required Facts

- The specific material copied and the exact challenged use.
- Whether the secondary use targets, comments on, or otherwise requires the
  original material.
- The degree of difference between the original and secondary purposes.
- The commercial or nonprofit character of the challenged use.
- The amount used, market effects, licensing markets, and all other statutory
  factors.
