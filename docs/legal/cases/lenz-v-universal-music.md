## Question Presented

What must a copyright holder consider before sending a takedown notification
that states a good-faith belief that the challenged material is not authorized
by the copyright owner, its agent, or the law?

## Verified Holding And Record

The Ninth Circuit held that § 512(c)(3)(A)(v) requires a copyright holder to
consider fair use before sending a takedown notification. Fair use is a
noninfringing use authorized by law, so it is part of the required good-faith
assessment rather than a matter that may always be deferred until after removal.

The court applied a subjective good-faith standard. The relevant question was
not whether a court would ultimately find the home video fair, but whether the
copyright holder actually formed a good-faith belief that the use was not fair.
The court held that a triable issue existed under an actual-knowledge theory.

The court also held that willful blindness can establish knowledge for a
§ 512(f) misrepresentation in an appropriate case. The plaintiff could not
proceed on that theory on the reviewed record because she did not show that the
copyright holder subjectively believed there was a high probability of fair use
and deliberately avoided learning that fact.

A person injured by a qualifying § 512(f) misrepresentation may seek nominal
damages even when the injury cannot be quantified as substantial economic loss.
The court did not decide ultimate liability because the appeal reviewed the
denial of cross-motions for summary judgment.

## Limits And Contrary Reasoning

- The opinion did not decide that the home video was fair use.
- The subjective standard does not impose liability merely because a court later
  disagrees with the copyright holder's fair-use assessment.
- A copyright holder cannot satisfy the statute by ignoring fair use or merely
  claiming good faith when contrary evidence supports knowing
  misrepresentation.
- The decision does not eliminate the statutory elements of injury, knowledge,
  materiality, causation, or damages under § 512(f).
- Judge Milan D. Smith concurred in part and dissented in part. He would have
  entered summary judgment for the plaintiff on the good-faith issue.

## Repository Relevance

A gameplay-video creator may have a supportable fair-use position and still face
platform removal, automated claims, or a disputed notice. SHAR documentation
must therefore separate the merits of the upload from platform procedure,
notice sufficiency, counter-notice disclosures, and litigation risk.

The case does not authorize game-file distribution, guarantee restoration,
prevent Content ID action, or make a counter-notice safe. A creator receiving a
notice should preserve the exact upload, notice, policy version, license
evidence, and editing record before making consequential representations.

## Required Facts

- The exact material identified in the notice and the challenged use.
- The copyright holder's pre-notice fair-use process and actual belief.
- The notice statements, platform action, resulting injury, and causation.
- The counter-notice content, personal disclosures, and any filed court action.
- The controlling jurisdiction and materially different circuit authority.
