- As-of date: 2026-07-16.

## Question Presented

Did a universal replacement transmitter violate § 1201(a)(2) by activating a
copyrighted computer program in a consumer-owned garage-door opener through a
rolling-code compatibility process?

## Verified Holding And Record

The Federal Circuit affirmed summary judgment for the replacement-transmitter
manufacturer. It held that the claimant bore the burden of proving the statutory
anti-trafficking elements, including that access enabled by the accused product
was without the copyright owner's authority.

The homeowners' use was presumptively authorized on the reviewed record. The
opener was sold without an agreement or other restriction forbidding use of a
competing replacement transmitter. The court rejected the argument that the
product owner had to prove express authorization for an ordinary use of the
purchased system.

The court described § 1201 as creating statutory causes of action rather than a
new property right. For the Federal Circuit's § 1201(a)(2) framework, the
claimant also had to establish a connection between unauthorized access and
infringement of a right protected by copyright. The claimant neither alleged
copyright infringement nor explained how the replacement transmitter
facilitated infringement. The required access-and-protection nexus was therefore
not established.

The court identified a claimant's prima facie burden as including ownership of a
valid copyrighted work, an effectively access-controlling measure, access made
possible by circumvention, lack of authorization, a connection to infringement,
trafficking by the defendant, and absence of an applicable statutory exception.
Only after the claimant establishes the required elements does a defendant bear
the burden for an affirmative statutory defense.

## Limits And Circuit Disagreement

- The opinion did not hold that every replacement part, universal transmitter,
  compatibility device, or circumvention product is lawful.
- Authorization depended on the sale, ordinary consumer expectations, absence of
  a contrary restriction, and exact use of the purchased system.
- The Federal Circuit's access-to-infringement nexus is not a nationwide rule.
  *MDY Industries, LLC v. Blizzard Entertainment, Inc.* expressly rejected that
  nexus in the Ninth Circuit and treated § 1201(a) access-control liability as
  distinct from infringement.
- *Lexmark International, Inc. v. Static Control Components, Inc.* reached a
  separate Sixth Circuit result because the authentication sequence did not
  control access to otherwise unavailable program code.
- The court did not decide that a purchaser can ignore an enforceable agreement,
  use unauthorized credentials, access a private service, or distribute copied
  software.
- The opinion addressed § 1201(a)(2), not every subsection, exception, remedy,
  jurisdiction, or later technological measure.

## Repository Relevance

SHAR must identify the protected work, measure, ordinary authorized route,
contractual restrictions, ownership status, accused technology, and alleged
copyright harm before assigning anti-circumvention risk. Compatibility or
activation alone does not establish every statutory element.

The authority is particularly relevant to replacement components, local
hardware compatibility, and independently created adapters. It does not govern a
private online-service bypass merely because both systems contain software. A
workflow involving accounts, product keys, private endpoints, copied content, or
protected service features presents materially different facts.

## Required Facts

- The copyrighted work and exact technological measure.
- Whether the measure effectively controls access to that work.
- The ordinary authorized access and use expected after the transaction.
- Every agreement, restriction, notice, assent event, and ownership fact.
- The accused product's design, functions, uses, marketing, and distribution.
- The access enabled and whether it is authorized in the controlling circuit.
- The copyright right allegedly infringed or facilitated, when the jurisdiction
  requires that connection.
- Applicable exceptions, defenses, later authority, and circuit law.
