## Question Presented

When may a possessor qualify as the owner of a computer-program copy under 17
U.S.C. § 117(a)(1), and which adaptations may be essential to using that program
with a machine?

## Verified Holding And Record

The court affirmed summary judgment for the business that possessed and modified
eight custom programs. Formal title was not an absolute prerequisite to owning a
copy for § 117. The inquiry instead asks whether the possessor exercises enough
incidents of ownership to be sensibly treated as the owner of that copy.

The business qualified on the aggregate facts: it paid substantial consideration
for programs developed for its sole benefit, stored the copies on its own server,
held an indefinite and materially unrestricted right to possess and use them,
and could discard or destroy them at will. Copyright ownership remained with the
author and was legally distinct from ownership of the particular program copies.

The court held that qualifying essential-step adaptations included fixing bugs,
updating customer and business information, adapting the programs to a new
computer system, and adding features that kept the programs useful for the
business purpose for which they were created. An adaptation did not need to be
strictly indispensable to booting or running the software.

The `used in no other manner` condition depended on the contemplated use. Access
by subsidiaries and limited direct client access remained within the same manner
of use because the programs continued to process the business transactions for
which they had been designed.

## Limits And Later Treatment

- The programs were custom-developed for one business, not mass-market software
  transferred under a written end-user license.
- Ownership depended on indefinite possession, broad use rights, substantial
  consideration, sole-benefit development, and the power to discard the copies.
- The decision does not make every lawful possessor an owner or authorize an
  adaptation used for a new purpose, transferred to others, or distributed.
- *Vernor v. Autodesk, Inc.* distinguished this record when applying the Ninth
  Circuit's license-designation, transfer-restriction, and use-restriction test.
- Denial of certiorari does not express a Supreme Court view on the merits.
- Contract, access-control, trade-secret, patent, and rights in non-program
  audiovisual material remain separate questions.

## Repository Relevance

SHAR must examine the complete transaction rather than treating either a
physical medium or the word `license` as conclusive. Copy ownership, copyright
ownership, and lawful possession are distinct. A local adaptation has the
strongest § 117 analogy when the user owns the relevant copy under controlling
law, the change maintains or improves use for the copy's established purpose,
and the adapted copy is not used or distributed in another manner.
