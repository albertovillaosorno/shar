## Question Presented

How did the Ninth Circuit assess intermediate BIOS copying during development of
an independently created emulator, and what later history is officially
verified?

## Verified Holding

The intermediate copies Connectix made and used while reverse engineering the
Sony BIOS were protected fair use, "necessary to permit Connectix to make its
non-infringing Virtual Game Station function with PlayStation games," and any
other intermediate copies did not support injunctive relief. The court applied
*Sega*: copying that would otherwise infringe can be fair use when it is
necessary to gain access to the functional, unprotected elements of the
software. 203 F.3d at 602-04 (citing 977 F.2d at 1518-19, 1524-26).

## Verified Factor Analysis

- Nature of the work: the BIOS "lies at a distance from the core" of copyright
  protection because it contains unprotected aspects that cannot be examined
  without copying; the factor favored Connectix. Id. at 603.
- Amount used: Connectix disassembled parts of the BIOS and copied the whole
  BIOS multiple times, so the factor weighed against Connectix, but it carries
  very little weight where the final product contains no infringing material.
  Id. at 605-06.
- Purpose and character: the district court applied an erroneous legal standard
  to commercial purpose; the Virtual Game Station is "modestly transformative"
  because it creates a new platform for game play. Id. at 606-07.
- Market effect: as a transformative product, the emulator "is a legitimate
  competitor in the market for platforms," and some economic loss to Sony from
  that competition does not compel a finding of no fair use. Id. at 607.

## Verified Remedy

The court dissolved the district court's preliminary injunction, remanded, and
reversed the finding that the Virtual Game Station tarnished the PlayStation
mark: "REVERSED AND REMANDED." Id. at 610.

## Verified Later History

The Supreme Court's official bound volume records denial of Sony's petition for
a writ of certiorari, docket No. 00-11, on October 2, 2000. That denial left the
Ninth Circuit judgment undisturbed but did not express Supreme Court approval of
its reasoning.

Later official Ninth Circuit materials continue to cite *Connectix* as software
fair-use authority. Their use of the case supports its continuing force within
the circuit while preserving the limits of the original facts and later
controlling authority.

## Not Established

- That every emulator, parser, or substitute product is lawful.
- That intermediate copying is automatically fair use.
- That a competitive final product necessarily favors or defeats fair use.
- That the decision resolves contract, patent, trademark, trade-secret, or
  anti-circumvention questions.
- That denial of certiorari creates Supreme Court precedent.

## Repository Relevance

Any SHAR analogy requires evidence about the copied material, necessity,
alternatives, development process, independently authored implementation, final
output, market effect, access controls, agreements, and governing jurisdiction.
