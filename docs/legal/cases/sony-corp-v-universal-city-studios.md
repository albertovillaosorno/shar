## Question Presented

Were manufacturers and sellers of home video recorders contributorily liable
because consumers used the devices to record copyrighted television programs,
and was private noncommercial home time-shifting fair use on the reviewed
record?

## Verified Holding And Record

The Supreme Court held that selling the Betamax video recorder did not establish
contributory infringement. The sale of copying equipment, like another article
of commerce, does not create contributory liability when the product is widely
used for legitimate purposes or is merely capable of substantial noninfringing
uses.

The record established substantial authorized uses because many copyright owners
permitted private recording of their freely broadcast programs. A product owner
could not use copyright in one set of programs to prevent consumers from using
the equipment for broadcasts whose owners authorized recording.

The Court also held that unauthorized private, noncommercial home time-shifting
of the respondents' freely broadcast programs was fair use on that record. The
viewers recorded programs they could not watch when broadcast and watched them
later without charge. The Court treated the private noncommercial purpose as
important and upheld findings that respondents had not shown a likelihood of
more than minimal market harm.

The decision addressed time-shifting rather than building a permanent library,
public distribution, retransmission, or creating a substitute commercial
service. It did not grant a general right to copy every work for personal use.

## Product Rule And User Conduct

The substantial-noninfringing-use rule addressed the manufacturers' secondary
liability for distributing a general consumer product. It did not declare every
consumer recording lawful, immunize a product's marketing or design, or replace
analysis of direct infringement by users.

The product's authorized and fair uses mattered. The Court rejected liability
based only on constructive knowledge that some customers might make unauthorized
copies where the manufacturers' contact with consumers was the ordinary product
sale and the recorder had substantial legitimate uses.

Later authority must be reviewed for inducement, active encouragement, product
design, services, continuing relationships, and other materially different
conduct. Labels such as `general purpose`, `interoperable`, or `user
controlled` do not establish the rule's conditions.

## Fair-Use Limits

- The time-shifting use occurred privately in the home and was noncommercial.
- The programs were already broadcast freely to the public.
- Recording enabled later viewing rather than a new public audience.
- The Court relied on the reviewed evidence about authorization and market harm.
- Copying entire programs did not defeat fair use on those facts because
  complete recording was necessary for the time-shifting purpose.
- The decision does not authorize uploading, streaming, distributing, selling,
  archiving, modifying, or publicly performing the recording.
- A paid service, on-demand library, remote retransmitter, or platform upload
  presents different purposes, audiences, copies, and markets.
- Fair use remains a four-factor, act-specific inquiry.

## Repository Relevance

SHAR cannot treat private time-shifting as authority to publish game footage,
recordings, extracted assets, converted builds, or complete audiovisual works.
Local private recording for later viewing differs from providing copies or
streams to others.

A distributed tool also requires evidence of legitimate uses, actual design,
marketing, documentation, defaults, support, and operator involvement. The
existence of one lawful use does not automatically resolve direct infringement,
inducement, access-control, contract, or platform-policy risk.

## Required Facts

- The product or service and every function, default, and marketed use.
- Authorized, fair, licensed, and infringing uses supported by evidence.
- The provider's knowledge, intent, encouragement, support, and continuing role.
- The user's exact recording, storage, viewing, retention, and distribution.
- Whether the source was freely broadcast, paid, access-controlled, or licensed.
- The purpose, commercial context, amount copied, and market evidence.
- Direct and secondary liability theories analyzed separately.
- Later controlling authority and governing jurisdiction.
