# UMG Recordings, Inc. v. Augusto

- Court: United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit.
- Authority: 628 F.3d 1175 (9th Cir. 2011).
- Docket: 08-55998.
- Decision date: 2011-01-04.
- Disposition: Summary judgment for the downstream reseller affirmed.
- As-of date: 2026-07-16.
- Counsel review: Not performed.

## Question Presented

Did a record company retain ownership of unsolicited promotional CDs through
labels stating that the discs were licensed, for promotional use only, and not
for resale?

## Verified Holding And Record

UMG mailed specially produced promotional CDs without a prior request or
agreement to music critics, radio programmers, and other selected recipients.
UMG did not charge for the discs, number or track the particular copies, require
reports about their use, or generally require their return. Troy Augusto later
acquired some of those physical CDs from downstream sources and sold them.

The Ninth Circuit held that UMG's distribution transferred title to the
recipients. The recipients therefore owned the particular phonorecords, and
§ 109(a) permitted later owners to sell those copies without UMG's permission.
A first sale can arise through a gift or another transfer of title; payment is
not required.

The result did not turn solely on the words printed on the discs. A short label
stating `Promotional Use Only—Not for Sale` did not purport to create a license.
The more detailed label asserted that acceptance created a license, but the
record contained no evidence that recipients assented merely by receiving
unsolicited copies.

UMG also lacked meaningful continuing control after shipment. It did not know
where most copies were, did not track their use, and did not require their
return. Under the complete circumstances, UMG retained too few incidents of
ownership to remain the owner of the physical copies.

## Unordered-Merchandise Context

The court also relied on 39 U.S.C. § 3009. The promotional CDs were unordered
merchandise because they were sent without the recipients' prior request or
consent. That statute allowed the recipients to treat the merchandise as gifts
and to retain, use, discard, or dispose of it without an obligation to UMG.
Those statutory rights were inconsistent with UMG's asserted transfer
restrictions.

Augusto was not a direct recipient and could not invoke § 3009 as though UMG had
mailed the discs to him. The statute mattered because it gave the original
recipients ownership and disposal rights, allowing title to pass downstream.

## Relationship To Software-License Cases

The court distinguished [*Vernor v. Autodesk, Inc.*](vernor-v-autodesk.md) and
other software cases. Those cases involved users who ordered and paid for
software subject to licensing arrangements. UMG's recipients had not requested
the CDs, assented to a transaction, or accepted tracked return obligations.

The *Vernor* considerations remain relevant to ordered software transactions.
*UMG Recordings* does not hold that every `license`, `not for resale`, or
promotional label transfers ownership. The transaction, assent, restrictions,
control, return duties, and governing statutes must be analyzed together.

## Limits And Unresolved Questions

- The case concerns ownership and resale of particular physical promotional CDs.
- It does not authorize reproduction, ripping, uploading, transmission, public
  performance, adaptation, or digital resale that creates another copy.
- It does not establish that every free sample is unordered merchandise.
- It does not eliminate valid licenses formed through actual agreement.
- It does not decide ordered software, account entitlements, subscriptions,
  cloud access, access controls, or retained duplicate files.
- A restrictive label remains relevant evidence even when it is not dispositive.
- Contract, consumer, postal, trademark, and foreign-law issues require separate
  analysis.

## Repository Relevance

SHAR must distinguish a label from an operative agreement. Wording such as
`promotional`, `property of`, `licensed`, or `not for resale` does not by itself
establish whether the holder owns or licenses a particular copy.

For each physical disc, cartridge, manual, promotional item, or bundled medium,
preserve how it was requested, delivered, accepted, tracked, paid for, returned,
or transferred. Unsolicited possession with no assent or continuing control can
present materially different facts from a purchased software copy governed by
accepted restrictions.

Even when first sale permits disposal of a physical copy, SHAR must not retain a
duplicate, publish its contents, distribute extracted assets, or treat ownership
of the medium as ownership of copyright.

## Required Facts

- The exact physical copy, markings, packaging, sender, and recipient.
- Whether the recipient requested or consented to the shipment.
- The words and presentation of every asserted license or restriction.
- Any affirmative assent, prior dealings, payment, return duty, or use report.
- Copy numbering, tracking, recall rights, destruction duties, and enforcement.
- The complete downstream acquisition and transfer history.
- Every reproduction, extraction, adaptation, upload, or retained duplicate.
- Governing copyright, contract, postal, consumer, and territorial law.
