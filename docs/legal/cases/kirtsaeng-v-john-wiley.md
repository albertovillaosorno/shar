# Kirtsaeng v. John Wiley & Sons, Inc

## Question Presented

Does § 109(a)'s first-sale rule apply to a particular copy of a United States
copyrighted work that was lawfully manufactured and first sold abroad with the
copyright owner's authorization?

## Verified Holding And Record

The Supreme Court held that § 109(a) applies to copies lawfully made abroad. The
phrase `lawfully made under this title` imposes a lawful-making requirement, not
a geographic limit restricting first sale to copies manufactured in the United
States.

The copyright owner authorized foreign manufacture and sale of the textbooks at
issue. The later owner imported and resold those particular physical copies. The
Court held that first sale limited the copyright owner's § 106(3) distribution
right and therefore also limited the related § 602(a)(1) importation claim.

The Court relied on statutory language, context, first-sale history, and the
practical consequences of permanent downstream distribution control over
foreign-made books and other goods. It did not treat a foreign-manufacturing
location as preserving an indefinite copyright veto over resale of a lawfully
made particular copy.

## Limits And Separate Rights

- The decision concerns the owner of a particular lawfully made copy and the
  right to sell or otherwise dispose of possession of that copy.
- It does not authorize reproduction, adaptation, extraction, transmission,
  public performance, or distribution of additional copies.
- It does not establish ownership where a transaction creates only a license,
  lease, account entitlement, or service-access right.
- A pirated, unauthorized, or unlawfully made copy does not receive first-sale
  protection merely because a person later possesses or purchases it.
- Ownership of a physical medium does not transfer copyright in the software,
  audiovisual works, music, characters, documentation, or other expression.
- Contract claims between parties, consumer law, import regulation outside the
  addressed copyright claim, and access-control restrictions require separate
  analysis.
- The case does not hold that a purchaser may retain a copy while transferring a
  duplicate, modified build, extracted payload, key, or digital image of it.
- The dissent would have read § 109(a) more narrowly in relation to foreign-made
  copies; the majority's non-geographic interpretation controls federal
  copyright analysis.

## Repository Relevance

A user may be able to resell or give away a particular lawfully made physical
game copy even when it was manufactured abroad. That conclusion does not
authorize SHAR to copy the disc into public history, distribute extracted or
converted content, publish a modified build, or treat a platform license as
ownership of a transferable copy.

Every workflow must identify the particular copy, lawful manufacture, ownership
transaction, retained duplicates, and exact disposition. First sale is a limit
on the distribution right for that copy, not a general interoperability,
modification, preservation, or publication exception.

## Required Facts

- The particular physical or digital copy and where and how it was made.
- Copyright-owner authorization for manufacture and the acquisition chain.
- Whether the user owns the copy or holds only a license or service entitlement.
- The exact sale, gift, lending, importation, or other disposition proposed.
- Every reproduction, installation, adaptation, extraction, or retained copy.
- Applicable contract terms, access controls, platform rules, and jurisdiction.
- Whether a separate asset, account, service, or protected work is involved.
