# Capitol Records, LLC v. ReDigi Inc

- Docket: 16-2321.
- Decision date: 2018-12-12.
- Disposition: Judgment affirmed.
- As-of date: 2026-07-16.
- Counsel review: Not performed.

## Question Presented

Did a digital-music resale system avoid the reproduction right and qualify for
§ 109(a) first-sale protection by copying data to new storage while deleting the
corresponding data from the seller's device?

## Verified Holding And Record

The Second Circuit held that ReDigi version 1.0 infringed the copyright owners'
§ 106(1) reproduction right. The system transferred a lawfully purchased digital
music file to ReDigi's server and, after resale, to the buyer's device or cloud
locker. Fixing the file in each new storage location created a new phonorecord
and therefore a reproduction.

Deletion of corresponding packets from the seller's device did not change that
result. The complete file did not need to exist in two locations simultaneously.
Receipt and storage in a new material object for more than transitory duration
created a new phonorecord even when the amount of data existing throughout the
process remained constant.

That persistent destination storage differs from the automatically overwritten
buffers in *Cartoon Network LP, LLLP v. CSC Holdings, Inc.* The Second Circuit
there held that buffers lasting no more than 1.2 seconds failed the duration
requirement on their fact-specific record.

Section 109(a) limits the distribution right for a particular lawfully made copy
or phonorecord. It does not address or excuse the creation of a new copy under
the reproduction right. Because version 1.0 necessarily made new phonorecords,
first sale did not protect those reproductions.

The court also rejected fair use. The system made unchanged, complete copies for
a commercial resale market that competed directly with the copyright owners'
market. Any limited technological convenience was outweighed by the lack of a
meaningfully different purpose and substantial market substitution.

## Limits And Unresolved Questions

- The holding concerns ReDigi version 1.0 and the technical record litigated for
  that system.
- The court did not decide whether ReDigi also infringed the § 106(3)
  distribution right.
- It did not decide whether version 2.0 or materially different transfer systems
  used by independent parties would infringe.
- It declined to decide whether a standalone digital file can itself qualify as
  a phonorecord independently of the physical storage in which it is fixed.
- The opinion did not hold that every Internet transmission or innocuous
  computer-created copy is infringing.
- Deleting or offsetting a source copy does not by itself authorize a new
  reproduction, but a separate statutory limitation or fair-use result may apply
  to materially different facts.
- The decision involved complete commercial substitutes for sound recordings;
  it does not determine every backup, migration, cloud-storage, preservation, or
  interoperability workflow.
- Contract, platform-license, access-control, and ownership questions remain
  separate.

## Repository Relevance

SHAR must not describe digital transfer as disposition of the same copy when the
workflow fixes protected content in new storage. Uploading an extracted game
file, generated asset, converted build, or complete protected payload to a
server or another user's device ordinarily creates a new material object and
requires a reproduction analysis before first sale can matter.

Deleting the local source after upload, streaming from an intermediate server,
or ensuring that only one usable instance exists at a time does not by itself
convert reproduction into transfer of the original material object. Repository
publication remains limited to independently authored or otherwise authorized
material.

## Required Facts

- The original material object, digital file, storage locations, and ownership.
- Every buffer, server, cloud locker, device, cache, and retained duplicate.
- When and for how long protected data is fixed in each location.
- Whether the process creates a new copy or phonorecord under controlling law.
- The exact source deletion, verification, and duplicate-detection behavior.
- The purpose, complete content used, commercial character, and affected market.
- The system version, jurisdiction, license terms, and later authority.
- Any separate statutory limitation, authorization, or fair-use theory.
