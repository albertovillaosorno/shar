## Question Presented

What did the Ninth Circuit decide about intermediate copying and disassembly
used to discover otherwise inaccessible functional compatibility information,
and how do later official authorities treat that decision?

## Verified Holding

The court held that disassembly of a copyrighted computer program is fair use as
a matter of law "when the person seeking the understanding has a legitimate
reason for doing so and when no other means of access to the unprotected
elements exists." 977 F.2d at 1514.

Before reaching fair use, the court reaffirmed that the Copyright Act does not
distinguish unauthorized copies by the stage of the infringer's work, so
intermediate copying can infringe section 106 regardless of the final product.
Id. at 1518-19 (citing *Walker v. University Books*).

## Verified Factor Analysis

- First factor: the commercial-purpose presumption of unfairness was overcome by
  the intermediate character of the use; the factor favored Accolade. Id. at
  1522-23.
- Fourth factor: it favored Accolade "notwithstanding the minor economic loss
  Sega may suffer," because the copying enabled independent creative expression
  rather than mere exploitation. Id. at 1523-24.
- Second factor: programs contain unprotected functional aspects unreachable
  without copying, so the factor favored Accolade. Id. at 1524-26.
- Third factor: Accolade disassembled entire programs, so the factor weighed
  against Accolade, but whole-work copying does not preclude fair use where the
  ultimate use is limited. Id. at 1526.

## Verified Trademark Holding

Where no other method of access to the console was known or readily available, a
rival's use of the initialization code did not violate the Lanham Act even
though it triggered a misleading trademark display, and Sega was primarily
responsible for any resulting confusion. Id. at 1514, 1528.

## Verified Remedy And Procedural History

The district court entered a preliminary injunction on April 3, 1992; the Ninth
Circuit stayed the recall order on April 23, 1992, and dissolved the preliminary
injunction on August 28, 1992, announcing that the opinion would follow. Id.
at 1517. The court dissolved the injunction as to both the copyright and
trademark claims, declined to direct relief for Accolade, assessed appellate
costs against Sega, and disposed of the appeal "AFFIRMED IN PART; REVERSED IN
PART; AND REMANDED." Id. at 1518, 1528, 1533.

## Verified Later History

The published Ninth Circuit opinion in *Disney Enterprises v. VidAngel* cites
*Sega* for the limited proposition that copying undertaken to study otherwise
inaccessible unprotected software elements may qualify as fair use. The court
distinguished that setting from copying and retransmitting protected
entertainment content.

A 2026 Ninth Circuit memorandum disposition also cites *Sega* in fair-use
analysis. The memorandum is nonprecedential and does not expand the original
holding, but it confirms that the case remains part of the circuit's current
analytical framework.

## Not Established

- A universal rule that reverse engineering or disassembly is fair use.
- A holding under the later-enacted DMCA or 17 U.S.C. § 1201(f).
- Permission to reproduce or distribute protected code, audiovisual material,
  audio, art, documentation, or other payloads.
- That commercial purpose or competitive effect is irrelevant.
- That the case overrides an applicable contract or another body of law.

## Repository Relevance

Any SHAR analogy requires evidence concerning lawful access, the functional
information sought, prior availability, necessity, alternatives, intermediate
copies, the independently authored final implementation, public output, market
effects, technological measures, agreements, and governing jurisdiction.
