## Question Presented

How did the First Circuit apply 17 U.S.C. § 102(b) to the Lotus 1-2-3 menu
command hierarchy, what did the Supreme Court disposition establish, and how
have later courts treated that reasoning?

## Verified First Circuit Holding

The majority framed the question as whether a computer menu command hierarchy is
copyrightable subject matter, addressed Borland's contention that the hierarchy
is "a system, method of operation, process, or procedure foreclosed from
protection by 17 U.S.C. § 102(b)," and considered the applicability of the
Second Circuit's *Altai* test before performing its own § 102(b) analysis. 49
F.3d at 812-14.

The court concluded: "we hold that the Lotus menu command hierarchy is
uncopyrightable subject matter," held that Borland therefore did not infringe by
copying it, declined to reach Borland's affirmative defenses, and reversed the
judgment of the district court. Id. at 819. Judge Boudin wrote a separate
concurring opinion emphasizing a different view of the underlying problem. Id.
at 819-22 (Boudin, J., concurring).

## Verified Supreme Court Disposition

The official United States Reports publication states that the First Circuit
judgment was affirmed by an equally divided Supreme Court. Justice Stevens did
not participate, and the Supreme Court Journal records denial of rehearing.

An equal division resolves the judgment between the parties but produces no
majority Supreme Court rationale. It must not be described as a nationwide
Supreme Court holding that software interfaces, menu commands, APIs, schemas, or
file formats are methods of operation.

## Verified Later Treatment

The Federal Circuit's 2014 *Oracle America v. Google* opinion discussed *Lotus*
while applying Ninth Circuit law. It stated that the Ninth Circuit had not
adopted the First Circuit's method-of-operation reasoning and treated that
reasoning as inconsistent with the binding Ninth Circuit precedent governing the
dispute before it.

The Supreme Court's 2021 *Google v. Oracle* opinion cited *Lotus* for software's
functional character and interface context but expressly assumed
copyrightability for argument's sake. The Court decided fair use instead and
therefore neither adopted nor overruled the First Circuit's copyrightability
reasoning.

## Not Established

- That every menu, data organization, command set, schema, API, or file format
  is a method of operation.
- That the Supreme Court adopted the First Circuit's reasoning nationwide.
- That labels, documentation, source code, examples, selection, coordination, or
  arrangement are never protectable.
- That later courts outside the First Circuit uniformly adopted *Lotus*.
- That the case resolves fair use, contract, patent, trademark, trade-secret, or
  anti-circumvention issues.

## Repository Relevance

Any reliance must identify the exact operation, the relationship between the
commands and that operation, technically available alternatives, expressive
choices, the controlling jurisdiction, and later binding authority.
