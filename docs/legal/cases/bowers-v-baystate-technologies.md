# Bowers v. Baystate Technologies, Inc

> [Legal Research Disclaimer](../disclaimer.md) applies to this record.

- Status: Reported Federal Circuit opinion text verified from a permitted
  secondary duplicate; an official archival PDF was not located.
- Court: United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit, applying First
  Circuit law to the contract and preemption questions.
- Authority: 320 F.3d 1317 (Fed. Cir. 2003).
- Decision date: 2003-01-29.
- As-of date: 2026-07-15.
- Counsel review: Not performed.

## Question Presented

Did federal copyright law preempt or narrow a contract claim based on a
shrink-wrap term that prohibited reverse engineering of licensed software?

## Verified Holding And Record

The court held that, under the First Circuit law it predicted would apply, the
Copyright Act did not preempt or narrow the contract claim. Mutual assent and
consideration supplied contractual elements beyond a copyright claim, and the
agreement bound its parties rather than creating an exclusive right against the
world.

The court preserved the separate copyright rule that reverse engineering object
code to identify unprotectable ideas can qualify as fair use. It nevertheless
concluded that private parties could contractually relinquish the limited
ability to reverse engineer recognized by copyright law when the agreement was
properly formed and enforceable.

The contract at issue unambiguously prohibited reverse engineering. Substantial
evidence supported the jury's finding that the defendant breached that term.
The court affirmed the contract verdict while avoiding duplicative recovery for
the same injury under copyright and contract theories.

## Limits And Contrary Reasoning

- The court predicted First Circuit law; it did not establish one uniform rule
  for every federal circuit, state, country, or agreement.
- The opinion assumed an enforceable agreement supported by assent and
  consideration. It does not make every shrink-wrap, clickwrap, or browsewrap
  term binding.
- Formation, notice, scope, unconscionability, public policy, statutory text,
  waiver doctrine, remedies, and later authority remain separate questions.
- *Vault Corp. v. Quaid Software Ltd.* reached a different result for a license
  restriction authorized by a Louisiana statute that conflicted with § 117. The
  *Bowers* majority distinguished that state-law scheme from the private promise
  before it.
- The decision did not hold that reverse engineering is copyright infringement
  whenever a contract prohibits it.
- The dissent would have found the reverse-engineering prohibition preempted and
  warned that mass-market terms could displace federal policies favoring access
  to unprotectable ideas.

## Repository Relevance

SHAR must not treat a copyright defense or interoperability purpose as an
automatic answer to contract risk. The exact acquisition path, agreement,
revision, assent evidence, governing law, restricted act, and contracting party
must be established before relying on or rejecting a reverse-engineering term.

The case also does not prove that a physical game purchaser accepted every term,
that a later service agreement governs an earlier disc transaction, or that a
contract can bind a nonparty. Those issues require transaction-specific facts
and controlling law.

## Required Facts

- The complete agreement and the exact reverse-engineering language.
- Notice, assent, consideration, parties, incorporation, and revision history.
- Governing law, forum, transaction type, and user location.
- The exact conduct alleged to breach the agreement.
- Statutory rights, public policy, preemption doctrine, remedies, and later
  authority in the controlling jurisdiction.
