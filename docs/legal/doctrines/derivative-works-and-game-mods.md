# Derivative Works And Game Mods

- Jurisdiction: United States baseline; other jurisdictions unresolved.
- Authority level: Cross-authority research record.

## Question Presented

What rights and liabilities may attach to a mod containing original additions
and preexisting game material?

## Verified Baseline

17 U.S.C. § 103 separates rights in new contributions from preexisting material
and addresses unlawful use. Authorization, fair use, ownership, originality,
separability, distribution, and remedies remain fact-specific.

The Ninth Circuit's game-mod decisions draw a narrow factual boundary rather
than declaring all modifications lawful or unlawful. In *Lewis Galoob Toys v.
Nintendo*, a consumer accessory made temporary runtime changes, altered no game
cartridge, created no independently usable display, and incorporated no
protected
work in a concrete or permanent form. The court found no derivative work and
also sustained an alternative fair-use analysis focused on private nonprofit use
and unproven market harm.

In *Micro Star v. FormGen*, commercially distributed level files permanently
described new audiovisual displays and stories that drew from the original
game's protected art, setting, and characters. The court found likely
derivative-
work infringement, rejected fair use, enforced the difference between free
sharing and commercial distribution, and characterized *Galoob*'s alternative
fair-use discussion as dicta.

The controlling comparison therefore requires the actual file and runtime
behavior: whether the challenged result is transient or permanently described,
what protected expression is incorporated, who creates and distributes it,
whether the use is commercial or transformative, the applicable permission, and
the effect on existing or reasonably likely derivative markets.

## Not Established

- That nearly all community mods are unlawful.
- That private or transient local modification is categorically fair use.
- That ownership of a game copy authorizes fixed or distributed derivative
  material.
- That a mod creator can never own or enforce original contributions.
- That technical dependency makes original material legally inseparable.
- That publisher-supplied tools or encouragement waive every commercial or
  derivative-work right.
- That one party's possible infringement authorizes copying by others.

## Required Facts

- Identity and source of every included asset and code component.
- Whether the challenged output is transient or permanently described, and what
  file, device, engine, or original work produces the rendered result.
- Which protected expression is copied, incorporated, referenced, or required.
- Whether the activity is private, public, distributed, commercial, or
  transformative.
- Permissions, licenses, platform rules, noncommercial limits, and applicable
  exceptions.
- Originality and separability of new contributions.
- Existing and reasonably likely markets for the original and derivative works.
- The challenged copying, procedural posture, governing jurisdiction, and
  requested remedy.

## Verification Checklist

- Build a component-level rights inventory.
- Separate original additions from preexisting material.
- Do not infer rights from another party's possible infringement.
