# Lawful Copy And Local Game Modification

> [Legal Research Disclaimer](../disclaimer.md) applies to this record.

- Status: United States framework verified; exact game and user result remains
  fact-specific.
- Jurisdiction: United States baseline; other jurisdictions unresolved.
- Authority level: Cross-authority research record.
- As-of date: 2026-07-15.
- Counsel review: Not performed.

## Question Presented

May a person who bought a physical game copy install it and modify the local
installation when the modified files are not sold or distributed?

## Short Answer

The facts can support lawful local modification, but there is no blanket rule
that every modification is lawful whenever the user owns a disc and does not
sell the result. The strongest United States basis is a combination of ownership
of the program copy, § 117's limited essential-step adaptation rule, any express
publisher authorization, and, where applicable, a fact-specific fair-use
analysis. The result weakens when the transaction is only a license, the change
copies protected expression beyond what use requires, access controls are
circumvented, contractual restrictions apply, or files are shared.

Copy ownership depends on the governing jurisdiction and complete transaction.
The Second Circuit's *Krause v. Titleserv* decision treated a custom-program
user as an owner where it paid substantial consideration, received programs
created for its sole benefit, held an indefinite and materially unrestricted
right to
possess and use the copies, and could discard or destroy them. The Ninth
Circuit's *Vernor v. Autodesk* decision instead treated a transferee as a
licensee where the copyright owner used a license designation, significantly
restricted transfer, and imposed notable use restrictions. Physical possession,
a purchase receipt, payment, and the word `license` are therefore relevant but
not independently conclusive.

Copyright and contract must then be assessed separately. *Vault Corp. v. Quaid
Software Ltd.* invalidated a state-act-backed decompilation restriction that
conflicted with § 117. *Bowers v. Baystate Technologies, Inc.* later enforced a
private reverse-engineering promise under the First Circuit law the Federal
Circuit predicted would apply and distinguished the statutory scheme in
*Vault*. Neither decision proves that a physical-copy purchaser accepted a
term, that a later service agreement governs an earlier disc transaction, or
that every jurisdiction would enforce the same restriction.

## Why Local Use Is Legally Different From Distribution

- Section 117 expressly addresses some copies and adaptations made by the owner
  of a program copy for use with a machine.
- *Krause v. Titleserv* treated repairs, routine data updates, migration to a
  new computer system, and features serving custom software's established
  purpose as qualifying adaptations on its record. It also required the adapted
  program to
  remain within the contemplated manner of use.
- *Lewis Galoob Toys v. Nintendo* treated temporary runtime changes that altered
  no cartridge and incorporated no protected display in permanent form
  differently from a fixed derivative package. Its alternative fair-use
  discussion was later characterized as dicta and is not a general exemption.
- *Micro Star v. FormGen* treated commercially distributed level-description
  files as likely derivative works even though the files referenced rather than
  embedded the game's art, because they permanently described protected displays
  and stories and entered the owner's derivative market.
- Keeping the change local can reduce the number of copies, recipients, and
  market-substitution concerns relevant to infringement and fair use.
- A patch containing only original instructions, offsets, or independently
  authored replacement material can present a different rights profile from a
  package containing original game code, models, textures, music, dialogue, or
  extracted assets.
- None of those facts eliminates contract or § 1201 analysis.

## Conditions Supporting The Narrow Position

- The operator lawfully acquired and retains the exact copy being used.
- Installation and adaptation occur only on the operator's machines.
- The modification does not defeat an access control or use unauthorized online
  services, credentials, or private endpoints.
- No original executable, asset, extracted payload, key, or modified build is
  uploaded or shared.
- Replacement content is independently authored or separately licensed.
- The exact game and platform terms do not supply a contrary contractual rule.

## Conduct Outside The Narrow Position

- Publishing the game, a cracked executable, a complete modified build, or
  extracted assets.
- Providing direct download links to unauthorized copies or proprietary
  payloads.
- Supplying keys, tokens, bypass instructions, or tools marketed for prohibited
  circumvention.
- Assuming that free, personal, educational, or noncommercial use is sufficient
  by itself.
- Treating physical-copy ownership as ownership of the copyrights embodied in
  the game.
