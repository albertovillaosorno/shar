# 17 U.S.C. § 1201(f) — Interoperability Reverse Engineering

## Question Presented

Does each claimed interoperability act satisfy every condition of § 1201(f),
including lawful right to use, sole purpose, necessity, prior availability,
independent creation, noninfringement, and restricted sharing?

## Verified Statutory Structure

Section 1201(f)(1) permits a person who lawfully obtained the right to use a
copy of a computer program to circumvent an effective access control applied to
a particular portion of that program solely to identify and analyze program
elements necessary for interoperability of an independently created program. The
elements must not previously have been readily available, and the acts of
identification and analysis must not constitute infringement.

Section 1201(f)(2) permits development and employment of necessary technological
means for that analysis or interoperability, subject to the same noninfringement
boundary.

Section 1201(f)(3) permits sharing acquired information or permitted means only
for enabling interoperability of an independently created program and only to
the extent that sharing does not constitute infringement or violate other
applicable law.

Section 1201(f)(4) defines interoperability as the ability of computer programs
to exchange information and mutually use the exchanged information.

*Davidson & Associates, Inc. v. Jung* applied these conditions to a game-service
emulator. The Eighth Circuit required lawful use rights, previously unavailable
information, a sole purpose of identifying and analyzing elements necessary for
interoperability, and noninfringing circumvention. It rejected the exception on
the reviewed record because the emulator bypassed product-key authentication,
did not validate unique keys, enabled protected online-service features, and
allowed unauthorized copies to use those features. The decision does not make
every emulator unlawful; it demonstrates that a general compatibility purpose
does not replace proof of every statutory condition.

## Act-By-Act Evidence Required

For every act, record:

1. The exact computer program and particular protected portion.
1. How and when the person lawfully obtained the right to use the copy.
1. The technological measure and why it effectively controlled access.
1. The precise circumvention act, if any.
1. The independently created program and the exchange or mutual-use objective.
1. Why the sought elements were necessary and not previously readily available.
1. Every intermediate copy and the infringement analysis.
1. Every technological means developed or employed and why it was necessary.
1. Every recipient, purpose, and legal basis for shared information or means.
1. Applicable contracts, access laws, trade-secret duties, and other law.

## Not Established

- That a general interoperability purpose satisfies the statute.
- That ownership of physical media proves a lawful right to use every program
  copy.
- That parsing an unencrypted file necessarily involves no technological
  measure, or that rejecting encryption conclusively resolves the issue.
- That every independently authored parser is an independently created program
  for the statutory purpose.
- That all information, code, commands, documentation, or tools may be shared.
- That § 1201(f) excuses infringement or violations of other applicable law.
