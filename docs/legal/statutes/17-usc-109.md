# 17 U.S.C. § 109 — Transfer Of A Particular Copy

- Status: Current statutory framework verified; application requires exact

- Jurisdiction: United States federal copyright law.
- Authority level: Federal statute.
- Statutory currentness: Text contains laws in effect on June 25, 2026.
- As-of date: 2026-07-16.
- Counsel review: Not performed.

## Question Presented

What does ownership of a lawfully made physical game copy permit, and does that
ownership itself authorize installation, modification, gameplay streaming, or
distribution of copied material?

## Verified Statutory Baseline

Section 109(a) permits the owner of a particular lawfully made copy to sell or
otherwise dispose of possession of that copy without the copyright owner's
permission. This is the first-sale rule. The rule limits the § 106(3)
distribution right for that particular copy; it does not transfer copyright or
authorize making another copy.

*Kirtsaeng v. John Wiley & Sons, Inc.* held that `lawfully made under this
title` does not impose a United States manufacturing requirement. Section 109(a)
can apply to a copy made abroad with the copyright owner's authorization. On the
reviewed record, first sale also limited the related § 602(a)(1) importation
claim because that claim depended on the § 106(3) distribution right.

*Adobe Systems, Inc. v. Christenson* allocates the evidentiary burdens in a
Ninth Circuit software-resale dispute. The first-sale proponent must initially
produce evidence sufficient to show lawful acquisition of ownership in genuine
copies. If the copyright owner responds that the original transfer was a license
rather than a sale, the owner must then establish that license or the absence of
an ownership-transferring sale. Actual admissible agreements matter; general
licensing testimony and generic templates did not prove the operative transfers
on that record.

*UMG Recordings, Inc. v. Augusto* held that unsolicited promotional CDs became
owned copies despite labels asserting a license, promotional use only, and no
resale. The recipients had not requested or agreed to the shipments, UMG did not
track or require return of the copies, and federal unordered-merchandise law
allowed the recipients to dispose of them. First sale can therefore arise from a
gift or another transfer of title, not only from a paid transaction.

*Capitol Records, LLC v. ReDigi Inc.* addressed a different problem. The Second
Circuit held that a digital-resale system created new phonorecords when it fixed
music files on a server and buyer's device. Deleting source packets and keeping
only one complete usable file at a time did not avoid the reproduction right.
Section 109(a) limits distribution of the particular lawful phonorecord; it does
not excuse creation of a new one.

Section 109(c) permits limited public display of that particular copy directly
or by projecting no more than one image at a time to viewers present where the
copy is located.

Those rules do not provide a general right to reproduce, adapt, transmit, or
publicly perform the work. Section 109(e)'s special public-performance rule is
limited to qualifying games in coin-operated equipment and does not create a
general home-game streaming exception. Ownership of the disc and ownership of
copyright in the software, audiovisual content, music, or characters are
different legal interests.

## Application Boundary

A lawful physical purchase is relevant evidence of ownership of that particular
copy. Manufacturing or sale abroad does not defeat first sale when the copy was
lawfully made with the copyright owner's authorization. But the claimant must
still establish ownership of the particular copy, not merely possession,
account access, a lease, or a software license.

Ownership can support the ownership element of § 117, but it does not by itself
resolve whether a proposed adaptation is essential to use, whether contract
terms apply, whether access controls may be bypassed, or whether a video upload
is authorized or fair use. In the Ninth Circuit, the copy-ownership inquiry
examines license designation, significant transfer restrictions, and notable use
restrictions; other jurisdictions may use a different test.

## Not Established

- That first sale authorizes copying game files or distributing a modified
  build.
- That a seller may retain a duplicate while transferring the original copy.
- That deleting source data or maintaining one usable instance prevents creation
  of a new copy in destination storage.
- That possession of a disc proves ownership of every installed program copy.
- That every foreign-made copy was lawfully manufactured or acquired.
- That noncommercial activity is automatically permitted.
- That gameplay transmission falls within § 109(c) or § 109(e).

## Required Evidence

- The physical medium, packaging, receipt, supplier invoice, manufacturing
  authorization, and complete transfer history.
- Evidence that the particular copy is genuine and was lawfully acquired.
- The actual original-transfer agreement when license status is asserted.
- Whether the copy was requested, accepted, tracked, returnable, or unsolicited.
- Whether the user owns the particular copy or holds only a license, lease,
  account entitlement, or service right.
- The exact copy disposed of and every duplicate or installation retained.
- The exact files copied or adapted and the reason for each act.
- The license, access-control, importation, publication, and distribution facts.
- Current controlling authority in the user's jurisdiction.
