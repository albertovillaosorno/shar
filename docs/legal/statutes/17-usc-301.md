## Question Presented

Which state-law contract, tort, unfair-competition, misappropriation, or other
claims concerning SHAR are expressly preempted by 17 U.S.C. § 301, and which
contain legally sufficient extra elements or otherwise remain outside
preemption?

## Verified Baseline

Copyright preemption and contract enforceability are claim-specific. The subject
matter, equivalence, extra elements, parties, remedies, and controlling circuit
must be analyzed separately.

*Bowers v. Baystate Technologies, Inc.* treated mutual assent and consideration
as extra elements that made the reverse-engineering contract claim qualitatively
different from a copyright claim. The court also emphasized that the promise
bound the parties rather than granting an exclusive right against the world. It
therefore predicted that First Circuit law would not preempt or narrow the
contract claim.

The dissent would have found the reverse-engineering prohibition preempted
because it displaced federal policy favoring access to unprotectable ideas. The
case therefore supplies an influential but contested circuit-specific analysis,
not a universal rule for every state-law claim or software agreement.

*Vault Corp. v. Quaid Software Ltd.* addressed a different state-law structure.
The Fifth Circuit held that a Louisiana statute authorizing licensors to
prohibit decompilation or disassembly conflicted with § 117 and was preempted.
That
conflict-based analysis should not be silently converted into a holding that
every private promise fails the current § 301 subject-matter and equivalence
tests.

## Not Established

This record does not establish that every reverse-engineering restriction is
preempted, that every contract survives preemption, that public policy
invalidates an agreement, or that one circuit's test controls nationally.

- Identify every alleged extra element and whether it changes the nature of the
  right.

- Record conflicting circuit approaches and later history.
- Obtain qualified counsel before asserting preemption.
