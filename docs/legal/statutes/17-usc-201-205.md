# 17 U.S.C. §§ 201 Through 205 — Ownership And Transfers

> [Legal Research Disclaimer](../disclaimer.md) applies to this record.

- Status: Current statutory framework verified; component-specific title
  requires exact instruments.
- Jurisdiction: United States federal copyright law.
- Authority level: Federal statutes, registrations, recordations, agreements,
  and controlling judicial interpretation.
- Statutory currentness: Text contains laws in effect on June 25, 2026.
- As-of date: 2026-07-14.
- Counsel review: Not performed.

## Question Presented

Who initially owned each relevant work, what written transfers or exclusive
licenses occurred, what was recorded, and who currently possesses the rights and
enforcement authority material to SHAR?

## Verified Baseline

Authorship, initial ownership, work-made-for-hire status, written transfer,
recordation, exclusive licensing, corporate succession, and enforcement standing
are distinct questions. A historical notice or product credit is not a complete
chain of title.

## Not Established

This record does not establish present ownership from a registration, merger
announcement, archived website, uploader identity, publisher credit, trademark
record, or possession of a copy alone.

## Required Facts

- Work identity, creation date, authors, employment or commissioning facts.
- Signed transfers, exclusive licenses, amendments, retained rights, and scope.
- Corporate mergers, asset purchases, dissolutions, and successor entities.
- Registration and Copyright Office recordation data.
- Territory, duration, media, sublicensing, and enforcement provisions.

## Required Authorities

- Current 17 U.S.C. §§ 201, 202, 203, 204, and 205.
- Copyright Office registration and recordation records.
- Signed agreements and authenticated corporate transaction documents.
- Controlling work-made-for-hire, transfer, standing, and recordation cases.

## Verification Checklist

- Build a separate chain for code, artwork, audio, music, performances,
  characters, localization, middleware, tools, and documentation.
- Distinguish ownership from license and enforcement standing.
- Preserve document dates, parties, operative grants, and unresolved gaps.
- Do not infer one chain for an entire product from one component's evidence.
- Obtain qualified counsel before relying on a chain-of-title conclusion.
