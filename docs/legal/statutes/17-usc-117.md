## Question Presented

Is the operator the owner of a copy of any relevant computer program, and do any
copies or adaptations made during installation, execution, maintenance, archival
work, repair, or analysis fall within 17 U.S.C. § 117?

## Verified Baseline

Section 117(a) permits the owner of a copy of a computer program to make or
authorize another copy or adaptation when it is created as an essential step in
using the program with a machine and is used in no other manner. It separately
permits an archival copy subject to destruction when continued possession
ceases to be rightful. Section 117(b) allows qualifying exact copies to transfer
with all rights in the program, but an adaptation prepared under § 117 may be
transferred only with the copyright owner's authorization.

This is the closest federal statutory rule to the proposition that the owner of
a lawfully acquired physical game copy may install and make some local technical
adaptations. It is not a general modding exemption. The operator must be the
owner of the program copy, the adaptation must satisfy § 117's purpose and use
limits, and separate game data, music, audiovisual material, contractual terms,
and access controls require independent analysis.

The federal courts do not apply one nationwide copy-ownership test. In the
Second Circuit, *Krause v. Titleserv* treated the possessor of custom-developed
programs as an owner where it paid substantial consideration, received programs
created for its sole benefit, held an indefinite and materially unrestricted
right to possess and use the copies, and could discard or destroy them. Formal
title was not indispensable on those facts.

In the Ninth Circuit, *Vernor v. Autodesk* treated a software transferee as a
licensee where the copyright owner used a license designation, significantly
restricted transfer, and imposed notable use restrictions. Neither a physical
medium, payment, indefinite possession, nor the word `license` is conclusive
outside the complete transaction and governing jurisdiction.

*Krause* also treated bug fixes, business-data updates, migration to a new
computer system, and features serving the program's established business purpose
as potentially essential-step adaptations. That holding involved custom business
software and does not convert every improvement, new purpose, or game-content
modification into a protected § 117 adaptation.

## Not Established

This record does not establish ownership of a copy, permission to distribute an
adaptation, authority to copy audiovisual assets, application to every licensed
program, or superiority over contractual restrictions.

## Required Facts

- Exact copy, acquisition transaction, medium, account, and license terms.
- Whether the operator owns or merely licenses the copy under controlling law.
- Exact copy or adaptation made and why it was essential or archival.
- Whether the copy or adaptation was transferred, shared, retained, or
  destroyed.
- Whether separate protected data, assets, or services were involved.

- Analyze ownership of each program copy separately.
- Distinguish program code from game data, media, documentation, and services.
- Record every copy, adaptation, transfer, and retention decision.
- Analyze contract and anti-circumvention issues independently.
- Obtain qualified counsel before relying on § 117.
